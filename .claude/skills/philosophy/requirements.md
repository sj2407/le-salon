# Le Salon — Parlor Philosophy Requirements

## Purpose

Each week, the Parlor introduces a philosophical movement ("courant philosophique") to spark conversation among intellectually serious friends. The entries must be substantive, historically grounded, and genuinely thought-provoking.

---

## Required Elements

### 1. The Era and Context

What was happening politically, culturally, and religiously when this philosophy emerged? Philosophy does not appear in a vacuum. Every movement is a response to specific historical conditions.

Questions to answer:
- What century and where?
- What political situation? (war, empire, revolution, collapse?)
- What was the dominant cultural mood? (optimism, anxiety, expansion, decline?)
- What religious or spiritual framework existed? (state religion, skepticism, reformation?)
- What material conditions shaped daily life? (plague, prosperity, urbanization?)

### 2. The Dominant Dogma

What ideas or beliefs held power before this philosophy emerged? What was the intellectual establishment? Every new philosophy argues against something.

Questions to answer:
- What did educated people believe before this?
- What institutions or authorities enforced those beliefs?
- What were the limits or failures of the previous worldview?
- Why had the old answers stopped working?

**Precision rule:** Do not lump multiple thinkers into a single claim. If Plato and Aristotle held different positions, say what each believed separately. Inaccuracy here undermines the credibility of everything that follows.

### 3. The Break

How does this philosophy contradict, revise, or depart from what came before? This is the drama. This is what makes it interesting.

Questions to answer:
- What specific claim or assumption does this philosophy reject?
- What does it propose instead?
- Why was this considered radical, dangerous, or liberating at the time?
- Who opposed it and why?

**The break must present the philosophy's actual argument, not just its conclusion.** Do not say "they rejected X and proposed Y." Show the reasoning: what question did they ask, what test did they apply, and why did the old answers fail that test? The reader should be able to follow the logic and understand why the conclusion follows.

**Every claim attributed to a named thinker must be verified against the actual source before inclusion.** This applies not just to critiques but to any secondary figure mentioned in the entry. If Rousseau is cited, read Rousseau. If the entry says "Aquinas argued X," confirm X against the SEP or primary text. Inaccurate paraphrases of other thinkers undermine the credibility of the entire entry.

### 4. The Core Claim

In plain language, what do these thinkers believe? What is the central insight? Avoid jargon. A smart friend who has never studied philosophy should understand this.

Questions to answer:
- If you had to state the position in two sentences, what would they be?
- What question is this philosophy trying to answer?
- What does it tell you to do, believe, or stop doing?

**Define technical terms before using them.** If the philosophy depends on a concept (e.g., "virtue," "indifferent," "will to power," "dasein"), the entry must define it in concrete terms before building on it. Do not assume the reader knows what the philosophers meant by a word that has a different everyday meaning.

**Distinguish carefully between related concepts.** If the philosophy draws a line between two things that sound similar (e.g., "value" vs. "goodness," "preferred" vs. "good"), make the distinction explicit and explain why it matters. This is where philosophical precision earns its keep.

### 5. The Founders and Popularizers

Who originated these ideas? Who developed them? Who made them famous? These may be different people in different eras.

Questions to answer:
- Who is credited with founding or first articulating this philosophy?
- Who were the major figures who developed it further?
- Who popularized it beyond academic circles?
- Was it influential in its own time, or rediscovered later?
- In what social class or profession did it find adherents?

### 6. Anecdotes That Do Philosophical Work

Anecdotes are not decoration. Every story in the entry must demonstrate a specific philosophical claim. If the anecdote does not clearly show what the philosophy gave its practitioner or what it looks like in action, cut it.

**Test each anecdote with these questions:**
- What philosophical claim does this story illustrate?
- Could a reader misinterpret this story in a way that contradicts the philosophy? If yes, the story needs framing or a different story should be used.
- Does the story show a complete arc? (Not just the dramatic moment, but what happened because of it.)

**The opening anecdote must include an explicit bridge to the philosophy.** After the story, a sentence or two must explain why this particular story sets up this particular philosophy. The reader should not have to guess the connection. The bridge should not be a generic statement ("This changed everything") but a specific claim about what the anecdote reveals about the thinker's perspective or method.

**Bad example:** "Epictetus's master broke his leg. He said 'I told you so.' He limped for the rest of his life." (This makes Stoicism look like a philosophy of accepting abuse. It stops at the dramatic moment without showing what the philosophy actually enabled.)

**Good example:** "Epictetus's master broke his leg. After gaining his freedom, he founded a school that drew students from across the Roman world. Stoicism gave him a framework in which his master's power over his body was irrelevant to the life he could build with his mind." (The story now demonstrates the philosophical claim: external circumstances do not determine the quality of a life built on trained judgment.)

### 7. The Defining Quote

One sentence or short passage that captures the spirit of the movement. This should be memorable and pithy. It should make someone want to know more.

Requirements:
- Must be from a primary source (the philosophers themselves)
- Should be comprehensible without extensive context
- Should provoke thought or reaction

### 8. The Key Works

Two to four texts someone could read to go deeper. Include the author and a one-line description if the title is not self-explanatory.

### 9. The Tensions and Critiques

No philosophy is complete or without problems. What are the internal tensions? What did critics say? What questions does it fail to answer or answer badly?

Questions to answer:
- What is the strongest objection to this philosophy?
- Where does it contradict itself or become unclear?
- What practical difficulties arise when you try to live by it?
- Who were its most serious critics and what did they say?

**Go to the source.** Do not paraphrase a paraphrase of a critic. Find what Nietzsche, Nussbaum, or whoever actually wrote and represent their argument accurately. A shallow or inaccurate critique is worse than none at all.

**Critiques should be substantive, not just labels.** Do not say "Nietzsche called this hypocrisy." Say what Nietzsche's actual argument was: what did he think the philosophy suppressed, denied, or got wrong about human life? The reader should feel the force of the objection.

### 10. The Contemporary Relevance

How do these questions show up in our lives now? This must be specific and substantive, not shallow. "How you check your phone" is not good enough.

Questions to answer:
- What current debates or dilemmas echo this philosophy's concerns?
- Where do we see this tension playing out in politics, technology, relationships, work?
- What would this philosophy say about a specific contemporary situation?
- Why might someone today find this useful or troubling?

**The closing question must do two things at once.** First, it must put the reader inside the philosophy's practice by describing a situation that mirrors what the philosophy demands of its practitioners. If the philosophy is about endurance, describe a moment of endurance. If it is about action, describe an action. If it is about withdrawal, describe a withdrawal. The reader should recognize themselves in the situation. Second, it must surface the tension that the philosophy's critics identified. The question should make the reader feel both the pull of the philosophy and the weight of the objection in the same moment. The reader should leave unsure whether the philosophy served them or gave them cover. Frame directly to the reader using "you." Use concrete situations readers have lived through, not abstract moral dilemmas they observe from the outside.

### 11. Sources

List all sources used in researching and writing the entry. This serves two purposes: transparency for the reader and a reference trail for future entries.

Format:
- Academic sources (SEP, IEP) with links
- Primary texts cited (with specific sections where applicable)
- Podcast episodes used (with links)
- Any other scholarly or historical sources

## Style Requirements

### Do:
- Start with a concrete scene, anecdote, or image
- Use specific names, dates, and places
- Let stories carry the philosophy (show, don't tell)
- Vary sentence length
- Trust the reader's intelligence
- Include genuine tensions and unresolved questions
- Write prose that rewards rereading
- Make every sentence do work (if a sentence could be cut without losing meaning, cut it)
- Match the register of the prose to the spirit of the philosophy. A Stoic entry can afford austere, muscular language. An Epicurean entry should feel lighter and warmer. A Cynicism entry might be blunt and provocative. The writing should feel like the philosophy it describes.
- Reserve "good," "bad," and other evaluative words for contexts where they carry philosophical weight (e.g., Plato's "the Good," Machiavelli's "to be good among so many who are not good"). In all other cases, replace with precise language: "serves," "damages," "helps," "harms."

### Do Not:
- Use em-dashes (—)
- Overuse commas and parentheses
- Use parallelism as a crutch ("not X but Y", "both A and B")
- Hedge with "perhaps," "arguably," "it could be said"
- Explain what the reader should think or feel
- Tie to contemporary life with shallow examples
- Write in a lecturing or pedagogical tone
- Use bullet points or headers in the final text
- Use portentous filler sentences ("The timing mattered." "This changed everything." "The implications were profound.")
- Use vague intensifiers instead of precise claims ("proposed something severe" — severe how? say what you mean)
- Use pseudo-profound formulations that state something obvious and pretend it is deep
- Use trite examples to illustrate philosophical claims ("A sick person can be wise. A rich person can be foolish." — of course they can. Use a real, specific example that does actual work.)
- Write sentences where the meaning is unclear or contradictory on a second reading ("never worth needing" — what does this mean?)
- Jump between paragraphs without transition (the reader needs connective tissue between the opening anecdote, the historical context, and the philosophical argument)
- Overstate claims about historical figures (an emperor is not helpless before fortune — be precise about what was and was not within his control)
- Collapse distinct thinkers into a single position to create a convenient foil
- Use flat adjectives that do no descriptive work ("good," "bad," "interesting," "important"). Replace with something specific. "The conversation was good" tells you nothing. "The conversation was honest" tells you something.
- Conflate how a philosophy was caricatured with what its critics actually objected to. When describing reception or suppression, distinguish between the stated objection and the actual threat. Christianity called Epicureanism hedonism. The real threat was its materialism.
- Use vague or hypothetical scenarios in closing questions ("When you watch someone you respect make a decision that is cruel but necessary" — nobody pictures themselves here. Describe a specific, recognizable situation the reader has been in, involving the kind of choice the philosophy actually asks its practitioners to make.)
- Use decorative phrasing that sounds literary but dissolves under scrutiny ("in a single gesture," "intentions unreadable," "the architecture of thought"). If a phrase could not be replaced with a plain-language equivalent that means the same thing, it means nothing.
- Contradict yourself across paragraphs. If paragraph A claims a philosopher holds position X, paragraph B must not claim the opposite without explicitly marking the shift. Read the entry as a sequence of claims and check that each one is consistent with what came before.

---

## Length

Target: 400-500 words for the main text, excluding quote, reading list, and sources.

---

## Process

1. Research the historical context thoroughly before writing
2. Identify the specific break with previous thought
3. Find 2-3 vivid anecdotes or scenes from primary or secondary sources
4. Locate the defining quote from primary sources
5. Identify the strongest critique or tension — go to the original critic's text
6. Draft the entry
7. **First review pass — Argument:**
   - Does every philosophical claim follow logically from what came before?
   - Are technical terms defined before they are used?
   - Could a reader misunderstand any anecdote? If yes, reframe or replace it.
   - Does every anecdote demonstrate a specific philosophical point?
   - Is the critique substantive and accurately sourced?
8. **Second review pass — Style:**
   - Remove all em-dashes
   - Reduce commas and parentheses
   - Cut every sentence that does not do work
   - Cut every portentous filler sentence
   - Replace every vague adjective with a precise claim
   - Check that transitions connect paragraphs
   - Verify closing questions are addressed directly to the reader
9. **Third review pass — Read aloud:**
   - Does it sound human or AI-generated?
   - Is any sentence confusing, contradictory, or clumsy on a second reading?
   - Would an intelligent reader learn something?
   - Does it invite conversation?

---

## Sources

For each entry, draw from:
- Philosophize This! transcripts (for anecdotes, accessibility, contemporary links)
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (for accuracy and depth)
- Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy (for accessible academic treatment)
- Primary texts (for quotes and specific arguments)
- Scholarly sources (for historical context)
- Original texts by critics (for accurate representation of objections)

Do not synthesize from general knowledge alone. Ground every claim in sources.
