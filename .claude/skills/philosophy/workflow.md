# Parlor Philosophy Research and Writing Workflow

## Overview

This workflow produces one philosophical movement entry for Le Salon's Parlor feature. Each entry must meet all requirements in PARLOR_PHILOSOPHY_REQUIREMENTS.md.

The goal is NOT to reproduce any single source's style. Philosophize This provides anecdotes and accessibility. Stanford Encyclopedia provides accuracy and the actual philosophical arguments. Wikipedia provides context and references. The final product synthesizes these into something new: substantive, literary, and concise.

---

## Phase 1: Source Gathering

For a given philosophical movement, collect these sources:

### 1.1 Academic Sources
- **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy**: Search for the movement name. Fetch the full article. Pay particular attention to the ethics section and how it presents the core argument step by step.
- **Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy**: Search for the movement name. Fetch if available.
- **Wikipedia**: Fetch the main article AND check the References section for primary sources and scholarly works.

### 1.2 Historical Context
- **Wikipedia**: Fetch articles on the historical period (e.g., "Hellenistic period", "French Revolution", "Weimar Republic")
- Look for: political situation, religious landscape, cultural mood, material conditions

### 1.3 Anecdotes and Accessibility
- **Philosophize This transcripts**: Map episode names to transcripts at philosophizethis.org/transcripts
- Extract: origin stories, vivid scenes, biographical details, memorable examples
- Do NOT copy style. Extract facts and stories only.
- **For each anecdote, note what philosophical claim it could illustrate.** An anecdote without a clear philosophical purpose will not be used.

### 1.4 Critiques
- Search specifically for critiques of the movement
- **Go to the original critic's text.** Do not rely on secondary summaries. Find what Nietzsche, Nussbaum, Marx, etc. actually wrote.
- Look in Wikipedia's "Criticism" sections for leads, then trace back to primary sources
- Search for famous critics by name (e.g., "Nietzsche critique of Stoicism", "Nussbaum critique of Stoicism")
- Fetch the relevant passages from the critic's own work (Project Gutenberg, academic sources, etc.)

### 1.5 Primary Sources
- Identify 2-4 key texts from the SEP or IEP articles
- Find one defining quote from a primary source

## Phase 3: Writing

### 3.1 Structure
Open with a concrete scene or anecdote. Move from the particular to the general. Let the philosophy emerge from the story.

Suggested flow:
1. Opening scene (50-75 words)
2. Historical context and dominant dogma (50-75 words)
3. The break and core claim, presented as an argument (75-100 words)
4. Key figures with anecdotes that demonstrate the philosophy in action (75-100 words)
5. Tensions and critiques, substantively sourced (75-100 words)
6. Closing question addressed directly to the reader (25-50 words)

Total: 400-500 words

Then add:
- Defining quote (with attribution)
- Reading list (2-4 titles)
- Sources list with links

### 3.2 Writing Rules Derived from Iteration

These rules come from specific problems encountered during the Stoicism and Epicureanism entries and apply to all future entries:

**Argument clarity:**
- Present the philosophy's reasoning, not just its conclusions. The reader should be able to follow the logic.
- Define every technical term before building on it. If the philosophy uses a word differently from its everyday meaning, say so explicitly.
- When the philosophy distinguishes between two similar concepts, make the distinction explicit and explain why it matters.

**Anecdote discipline:**
- Every anecdote must demonstrate a specific philosophical claim.
- Show the complete arc: not just the dramatic moment but what followed because of it.
- Ask: could a reader misinterpret this story? If the anecdote could make the philosophy look bad (e.g., "Stoicism taught a slave to accept violence"), it needs reframing or replacement.

**Historical precision:**
- Attribute claims to specific thinkers. Do not say "Plato and Aristotle believed X" if they believed different things.
- Be precise about what historical figures could and could not control. An emperor is not helpless before fortune. A slave is not merely enduring.
- When describing how a philosophy was received or suppressed, distinguish between the stated objection and the actual threat. Christianity called Epicureanism hedonism; the real threat was its denial of the soul, providence, and the afterlife.

**Critique quality:**
- Go to the original critic's text. Represent their actual argument, not a simplified label.
- The critique should have force. The reader should feel the weight of the objection.
- A critique like "Nietzsche called this hypocrisy" is a label. A critique like "Nietzsche argued that to lower your pain you must also lower your capacity for joy, and that suffering is the condition for growth" is an argument.

**Register and word choice:**
- Match the register of the prose to the spirit of the philosophy. An entry about Epicureanism should feel warm and light, not heavy and austere. An entry about Cynicism might be blunt and confrontational. The writing should embody what it describes.
- Do not use flat adjectives that do no descriptive work. "Good," "bad," "interesting," "important" tell the reader nothing. Replace with something specific. "The conversation was good" is empty. "The conversation was honest" has content.
- When a word or phrase could describe a different philosophy just as easily, it is not precise enough. "Stop trying to fix what you cannot control" sounds like Stoicism, not Epicureanism. Find the language that belongs only to the philosophy you are writing about.

**Transitions and flow:**
- Every paragraph must connect to the next. Do not jump from an anecdote to a historical claim without connective tissue.
- The opening anecdote must arrive at the historical context naturally.

**Closing:**
- Frame closing questions directly to the reader using "you" or "your."
- Do not use passive constructions. "Does this make injustice easier to tolerate" is passive. "Does this give you permission to endure what you should be refusing to accept" is direct.
- Each entry's closing question must be distinct from previous entries. If two philosophies face a similar tension (e.g., the risk of inaction), the questions should approach it from opposite directions. The Stoic question is about engagement. The Epicurean question is about withdrawal. They should not be interchangeable.

### 3.3 Style Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

**Argument**
- [ ] Every philosophical claim follows logically from what precedes it
- [ ] Technical terms defined before use
- [ ] Distinctions between similar concepts made explicit
- [ ] Every anecdote demonstrates a specific philosophical point
- [ ] No anecdote can be easily misread as contradicting the philosophy
- [ ] Critique is substantive and traced to the original source
- [ ] Historical reception distinguishes stated objections from actual threats
- [ ] Closing question addressed directly to the reader
- [ ] Closing question is distinct from previous entries' questions

**Style**
- [ ] No em-dashes (—)
- [ ] Minimal commas (read aloud to check)
- [ ] No unnecessary parentheses
- [ ] No "not only X but also Y" constructions
- [ ] No "it is not just X, it is Y" constructions
- [ ] No hedging: "perhaps", "arguably", "it could be said"
- [ ] No portentous filler: "The timing mattered", "This changed everything", "The implications were profound"
- [ ] No vague intensifiers: every adjective replaced with a precise claim
- [ ] No flat adjectives: "good", "bad", "interesting", "important" replaced with specific descriptors
- [ ] No pseudo-profound formulations that state the obvious
- [ ] No trite examples (replace with real, specific ones)
- [ ] No contradictory or unclear sentences (reread every sentence and ask: what does this actually mean?)
- [ ] No language that could describe a different philosophy just as easily (if a sentence sounds Stoic in an Epicurean entry, rewrite it)
- [ ] Register matches the spirit of the philosophy
- [ ] Transitions between every paragraph
- [ ] Varied sentence lengths
- [ ] Concrete over abstract

**Sources**
- [ ] Quote is from primary source
- [ ] All claims traceable to gathered sources
- [ ] Anecdotes verified in multiple sources
- [ ] Critiques traced to original texts
- [ ] Source list included with links

## Phase 4: Review

### First pass: Argument
Read the entry and ask:
- Can I follow the logical argument from premise to conclusion?
- Are there any moments where I have to guess what a term means?
- Could any anecdote be misunderstood?
- Does the critique have real force, or is it a label?

### Second pass: Style
Read the entry and cut:
- Every sentence that does not do work
- Every portentous filler sentence
- Every vague adjective (replace with a precise claim)
- Every sentence that is unclear or contradictory on a second reading

### Third pass: Read aloud
- Does it sound human or AI-generated?
- Is the contemporary relevance genuinely thought-provoking?
- Would an intelligent reader learn something?
- Does it invite conversation?
- Does the closing question stay with you?
