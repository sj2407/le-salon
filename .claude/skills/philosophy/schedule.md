# Le Salon — Parlor Philosophy: Master Schedule

## How This List Was Built

Each entry on this list passed three tests:

1. **Richness:** Does this movement have enough material for a full entry? That means: a dramatic origin story or historical moment, a clear break with what came before, at least two strong anecdotes, a substantive critique, and a question that would generate real conversation among smart people.

2. **Coherence:** Does this entry connect naturally to the ones before and after it? Each week should feel like the next chapter in an ongoing intellectual story, not a random topic.

3. **Balance:** Is there roughly equal weight across entries? No week should feel thin (a single idea stretched to fill space) or cramped (three major thinkers compressed into one entry).

Entries that failed these tests were either cut, split into multiple weeks, or absorbed into adjacent entries. The result is not a fixed number of weeks but a sequence that can be followed in order, paused, or extended.

## Arc 1: The Ancient Crisis (Weeks 1–3)

Alexander's empire collapses. The Greek city-states lose their independence. Three rival schools emerge, each proposing a radically different answer to the same question: how do you live when the world offers no stability?

### Week 1: Stoicism ✅ COMPLETE
**c. 300 BCE — Athens**
**The question:** How do you live well when everything you relied on has been taken away?
**The break:** Only virtue — wisdom, justice, courage, moderation — constitutes genuine good. Everything else is "indifferent."
**Key figures:** Zeno, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 11, 12, 237
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/

### Week 2: Epicureanism
**c. 300 BCE — Athens**
**The question:** If the gods don't intervene and death is the end, what is left to pursue?
**The break:** Pleasure is the highest good — but the greatest pleasure is the absence of pain and anxiety, not indulgence. Withdraw from public life into a community of friends.
**Key figures:** Epicurus, Lucretius
**Connection to previous:** Same historical crisis as Stoicism, opposite answer. The Stoics said engage but detach. The Epicureans said withdraw entirely. The contrast is the conversation.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 13, 14
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/
**Richness assessment:** Strong. Epicurus's Garden as a physical community, his Letter to Menoeceus, the "tetrapharmakos" (four-part remedy), Lucretius's poetry. Marx wrote his dissertation on Epicurus. Strong critique available from the Stoics and from Christianity (which demonized Epicureanism for centuries).

### Week 3: Cynicism
**c. 400–300 BCE — Athens**
**The question:** What if civilization itself is the problem?
**The break:** Convention, property, status, modesty — all of it is artificial. Live according to nature, which means stripping away everything society tells you to want.
**Key figures:** Diogenes, Crates (Zeno's teacher)
**Connection to previous:** Cynicism is the radical ancestor of both Stoicism and, to some extent, Epicureanism. Zeno studied under Crates. Understanding what the Cynics proposed (and why the Stoics pulled back from it) deepens both previous entries.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 10
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-ancient/#Cyn
**Richness assessment:** Strong on anecdotes (Diogenes is one of the most vivid characters in philosophy), but the movement is thin on systematic thought. Works best as a shorter, punchier entry focused on the provocation: what happens when you take "live according to nature" literally?

## Arc 2: Faith and Reason (Weeks 4–6)

The ancient world gives way to monotheism. Philosophy becomes entangled with theology. The central question shifts: what is the relationship between what you can know through reason and what you must accept on faith?

### Week 4: Augustine and Early Christian Philosophy
**4th–5th century CE — Roman North Africa**
**The question:** How do you reconcile Greek philosophy with Christian revelation?
**The break:** The inner life becomes the primary site of philosophical investigation. Augustine turns Platonic contemplation inward: truth is found not by looking at the world but by looking into the self, where God has left traces.
**Key figures:** Augustine, (Plotinus as precursor)
**Connection to previous:** The Stoics and Epicureans offered secular paths to the good life. Christianity absorbed and transformed Stoic ethics while rejecting Epicurean materialism. Augustine is the hinge.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 16, 17
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/augustine/
**Richness assessment:** Strong. Augustine's *Confessions* is one of the great autobiographies. His conversion story is dramatic. The tension between his Neoplatonic inheritance and Christian faith is genuine. Critique from Pelagius (on free will) and later from the Enlightenment.

### Week 5: Islamic Golden Age Philosophy
**9th–12th century — Baghdad, Córdoba, Cairo**
**The question:** Can Aristotle's philosophy be reconciled with the Quran? And what happens when reason and revelation genuinely conflict?
**The break:** Aristotle's works, lost to the Latin West, are translated, preserved, and radically developed by Islamic thinkers. Avicenna builds a complete philosophical system. Averroes argues that philosophy and religion describe the same truth in different languages. Al-Ghazali attacks them both: philosophy cannot reach God.
**Key figures:** Al-Kindi, Avicenna (Ibn Sina), Averroes (Ibn Rushd), Al-Ghazali
**Connection to previous:** Augustine fused Plato with Christianity. The Islamic philosophers did the same with Aristotle and Islam — and the internal debate (Averroes vs. Al-Ghazali) mirrors tensions that recur in every encounter between philosophy and faith.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 18 (briefly)
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-metaphysics/
**Other sources:** History of Philosophy Without Any Gaps (Peter Adamson) — extensive Islamic philosophy coverage; In Our Time episodes on Avicenna, Averroes, Al-Ghazali
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. Most Western-educated readers know almost nothing about this tradition, which makes it genuinely illuminating. The Averroes/Al-Ghazali debate is one of the great intellectual dramas. The historical fact that Aristotle reached Aquinas *through* Arabic translation is itself a conversation starter.

### Week 6: Scholasticism and Aquinas
**13th century — Paris, Cologne**
**The question:** Can you prove God's existence using reason alone? Can faith and philosophy coexist as equal partners?
**The break:** Aquinas argues that faith and reason are compatible because they come from the same source (God). He builds a systematic synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology. His "Five Ways" attempt to demonstrate God's existence through logical argument.
**Key figures:** Aquinas, Anselm (precursor), William of Ockham (critic)
**Connection to previous:** Aquinas could not have written the *Summa* without Averroes's commentaries on Aristotle. He explicitly engages with and argues against Averroes. This is a direct continuation.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 19, 20
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aquinas/
**Richness assessment:** Strong if focused tightly on Aquinas and the Five Ways. The critique from Ockham (who argued that God's existence cannot be proven by reason — only accepted by faith) is a clean, powerful tension. Risk: could feel dry if it becomes a theology lecture. Needs strong anecdotes about Aquinas's life (the "Dumb Ox" story, his sudden cessation of writing near death).

## Arc 3: The Modern Turn (Weeks 7–9)

The Renaissance and Reformation shatter medieval certainties. Philosophy separates from theology and asks: if tradition and authority can't be trusted, what method can replace them?

### Week 7: Machiavelli and Political Realism
**Early 16th century — Florence**
**The question:** What if politics has nothing to do with morality? What if the effective ruler must sometimes be willing to do evil?
**The break:** Machiavelli separates politics from ethics entirely. Virtue for a ruler is not goodness but effectiveness. The Prince is a manual for acquiring and maintaining power in a world where fortune is hostile and stability is fragile.
**Key figures:** Machiavelli
**Connection to previous:** Aquinas argued that political authority derives from divine law and must serve moral ends. Machiavelli says: that is a fantasy that gets rulers killed. This is the first great modern break — politics freed from theology.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 23
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/machiavelli/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. Machiavelli's biography is dramatic (political career, exile, torture, writing *The Prince* from disgrace). The controversy is evergreen: is he a cynical advisor to tyrants or an honest analyst of how power actually works? The "Machiavellian" label itself is a critique worth examining.

### Week 8: Descartes and the Method of Doubt
**Early 17th century — France, Netherlands**
**The question:** What can I know with absolute certainty if I start from nothing?
**The break:** Doubt everything — senses, memory, even mathematics. The one thing that survives is the act of doubting itself: "I think, therefore I am." From this foundation, rebuild all knowledge through reason alone.
**Key figures:** Descartes
**Connection to previous:** Machiavelli freed politics from theology. Descartes frees philosophy from theology. Both start from the assumption that inherited authority is unreliable and you must build from your own resources.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 28, 29, 30
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/descartes/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. The biographical details are vivid (the stove-heated room, the three dreams, the secrecy about his work). The mind-body problem he creates is still unresolved. Critiques from Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia (how does an immaterial mind move a material body?) are pointed and accessible.

### Week 9: Empiricism — Locke and Hume
**17th–18th century — Britain**
**The question:** What if reason alone can't give us knowledge? What if everything we know comes from experience?
**The break:** Locke: the mind starts as a blank slate, all ideas come from sensory experience. Hume takes this further: even causation is just a habit of expectation, not something we observe. Reason alone, separated from experience, gives us nothing about the world.
**Key figures:** Locke, Hume (Berkeley mentioned but secondary)
**Connection to previous:** Direct attack on Descartes. The rationalists said reason is the source of knowledge. The empiricists said experience is. Hume's radical skepticism about causation will later wake Kant from his "dogmatic slumber."
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 35–36 (Locke), 51–54 (Hume)
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/empiricism/
**Richness assessment:** Strong for Hume especially. His argument about causation is genuinely surprising and counterintuitive — perfect salon material. Locke's political philosophy (consent of the governed) connects to later entries. Risk: two thinkers in one week, but they're building the same argument in sequence so it works.

## Arc 4: Revolutions in Thought (Weeks 10–13)

The Enlightenment asks what a society built on reason would look like. The answers generate both democratic revolution and new forms of tyranny.

### Week 10: Rousseau and the Social Contract
**Mid-18th century — Geneva, Paris**
**The question:** If humans are naturally good, what made us miserable? And can a just society be built from scratch?
**The break:** Civilization corrupts. Property creates inequality. But we cannot go back to nature — so we must create a social contract where each person submits to the "general will," gaining civil freedom in exchange for natural freedom.
**Key figures:** Rousseau (Hobbes and Locke as predecessors, covered briefly)
**Connection to previous:** Hume showed that reason alone can't ground morality. Rousseau responds: morality comes from feeling, from natural compassion. And the political order must be rebuilt from the ground up.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 44–47
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/rousseau/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. Rousseau's biography is extraordinary (abandoning his children, quarreling with every friend, writing Confessions). The "general will" concept is both influential and deeply troubling — it inspired the French Revolution AND its critics accused it of laying the groundwork for totalitarianism. Voltaire's critique is sharp and witty.

### Week 11: Kant — Morality and Its Limits
**Late 18th century — Königsberg**
**The question:** How can morality be objective and universal if it doesn't come from God?
**The break:** Morality is grounded in reason and duty alone. Act only according to principles you could will as universal laws (the categorical imperative). Consequences are irrelevant to moral worth. Kant also limits what reason can know: we can never access things as they are in themselves, only as they appear to us.
**Key figures:** Kant
**Connection to previous:** Rousseau shook Kant out of his "dogmatic slumber." Hume's skepticism forced Kant to ask: what can reason actually do? Kant's answer: it can ground morality, but it cannot reach ultimate reality.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 56–63
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant-moral/
**Richness assessment:** Strong but risks being abstract. Needs to be anchored in the biographical detail (Kant never left Königsberg, his clockwork daily walks, citizens setting their watches by him) and in concrete moral dilemmas (is it wrong to lie to a murderer at the door?). The critique from consequentialists (sometimes following duty produces terrible outcomes) is accessible.

### Week 12: Hegel — History Has a Direction
**Early 19th century — Berlin**
**The question:** What if history is not random but a rational process driving toward freedom?
**The break:** Reality is not static but develops through contradiction (dialectic). Every idea generates its opposite; the clash produces a higher synthesis. History moves through this process toward the realization of freedom and self-consciousness. Philosophy's job is to comprehend its own time in thought.
**Key figures:** Hegel, (Fichte as precursor)
**Connection to previous:** Kant said we can never know things in themselves. Hegel said: that barrier is itself something reason can overcome. Where Kant set limits, Hegel broke through them — and in doing so, created the framework that Marx would later invert.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 66, 67
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hegel/
**Richness assessment:** Difficult but essential. Hegel is notoriously hard to explain clearly. The entry needs to focus on the dialectic and the idea that history has a direction — these are genuinely provocative for a salon audience. The critique from Kierkegaard ("Hegel built a palace of ideas and lived in a shack next door") is perfect. Risk: if the writing is not very clear, this week will lose people.

### Week 13: Utilitarianism — The Calculus of Happiness
**Late 18th–19th century — Britain**
**The question:** What if the right action is simply the one that produces the most happiness for the most people?
**The break:** Reject abstract duty. Morality is about consequences, measured in pleasure and pain. Every person counts equally. The greatest good for the greatest number.
**Key figures:** Bentham, John Stuart Mill
**Connection to previous:** Direct response to Kant. Kant said consequences are irrelevant to moral duty. Bentham said consequences are the *only* thing that matters. Mill refined this: not all pleasures are equal (it is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a pig satisfied).
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 41–43
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/utilitarianism-history/
**Richness assessment:** Strong. Bentham's character is vivid (he had his body preserved and displayed at University College London — it's still there). The "trolley problem" is an accessible way into the tension. Mill's *On Liberty* connects to free speech debates. Peter Singer as a contemporary utilitarian extends the conversation.

## Arc 5: The Demolition Crew (Weeks 14–16)

Three thinkers who systematically dismantle the certainties of the modern world: the economic order, the moral order, and the Christian God.

### Week 14: Marx — History from Below
**Mid-19th century — London, Brussels, Paris**
**The question:** What if the ideas that dominate a society are just the ideas of the ruling class?
**The break:** Philosophy should not merely interpret the world but change it. Consciousness is determined by material conditions. History moves through class struggle, not the progress of ideas (contra Hegel).
**Key figures:** Marx, Engels
**Connection to previous:** Hegel said history is the progress of Spirit toward freedom. Marx said: turn Hegel upside down. History is the progress of material conditions through class conflict. The utilitarians wanted to reform society to maximize happiness. Marx asked: whose happiness? And who controls the system that distributes it?
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 75–82
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/marx/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. Marx's biography is dramatic (poverty, exile, the British Museum reading room). The concept of alienation is immediately resonant for anyone who has worked a job they found meaningless. Critiques from Popper (unfalsifiable), Arendt (totalitarian consequences), and the historical record.

### Week 15: Nietzsche — The Death of God
**Late 19th century — Basel, Sils-Maria, Turin**
**The question:** If God is dead and all values are human inventions, what prevents everything from collapsing into meaninglessness?
**The break:** Christian morality is a "slave morality" that elevates weakness and punishes strength. The death of God leaves a void. The response is not despair but the creation of new values through the will to power. The highest human type, the Übermensch, creates meaning rather than inheriting it.
**Key figures:** Nietzsche
**Connection to previous:** Marx attacked the economic order. Nietzsche attacked the moral order itself. Both saw existing values as serving particular interests, but Nietzsche rejected Marx's collectivism. Where Marx saw liberation in solidarity, Nietzsche saw it in individual self-overcoming.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 90–93
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/nietzsche/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. Nietzsche's biography is extraordinary (the collapse in Turin, the years of silence). The writing is vivid and quotable. The misappropriation by the Nazis is a crucial historical footnote. Critiques: the will to power can license cruelty; the Übermensch can become a justification for authoritarianism.

### Week 16: Kierkegaard — The Leap of Faith
**Mid-19th century — Copenhagen**
**The question:** What if rational systems (Hegel) and moral duty (Kant) both miss the point? What if truth is subjective and the only authentic choice is a leap into the unknown?
**The break:** Existence cannot be captured in a system. The individual, facing anxiety and the absurd, must make a "leap of faith" that reason cannot justify. The three stages of existence: aesthetic (pleasure), ethical (duty), religious (faith beyond reason).
**Key figures:** Kierkegaard
**Connection to previous:** Kierkegaard attacked Hegel directly: you cannot systematize existence from outside it; you are always already inside. He also anticipates Nietzsche's focus on the individual — but where Nietzsche rejects faith, Kierkegaard embraces it as the highest act of freedom.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 97 (briefly, as precursor to existentialism)
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kierkegaard/
**Richness assessment:** Strong. The biographical details are vivid (broken engagement with Regine Olsen, pseudonymous authorship, attack on the Danish church). The "leap of faith" concept is immediately provocative. Pairs well as a bridge between Nietzsche and existentialism. Risk: less podcast material than other entries; will need heavier academic sourcing.

## Arc 6: The Crisis of Meaning (Weeks 17–20)

Two world wars shatter European confidence in progress, reason, and civilization. Philosophers ask: if systems collapse, what remains?

### Week 17: Phenomenology — Back to Experience
**Early 20th century — Germany**
**The question:** What does the world look like before we theorize about it? What are the structures of experience itself?
**The break:** "Back to the things themselves." Philosophy should describe how things appear to consciousness, not construct abstract systems. Intentionality: consciousness is always consciousness *of* something.
**Key figures:** Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty
**Connection to previous:** After Nietzsche and Kierkegaard dismantled the great systems, phenomenology tried to rebuild from the most basic thing: raw experience. What can you say about the world if you strip away all assumptions?
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 97–99 (Heidegger)
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/phenomenology/
**Richness assessment:** Moderate. Husserl is important but hard to make dramatic. Heidegger is dramatic (Nazi party membership) but dense. Merleau-Ponty is the most accessible (philosophy of the body). This entry works if it focuses on Heidegger's question — what does it mean to exist? — and uses his biography to raise the question of whether a philosophy can be separated from its philosopher's moral failures.

### Week 18: Existentialism — Radical Freedom
**Mid-20th century — Paris**
**The question:** If there is no fixed human nature and no predetermined purpose, how do you create meaning?
**The break:** Existence precedes essence. You are not born with a purpose — you must create one through action and commitment. Freedom is inescapable and terrifying. You are responsible for what you make of yourself, with no excuses.
**Key figures:** Sartre, de Beauvoir, Camus
**Connection to previous:** Heidegger asked what it means to exist. Sartre took that question into the moral and political arena. If Being has no given essence, you are radically free and radically responsible. De Beauvoir extended this to gender: "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman."
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 86–89
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/existentialism/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong. The Parisian café culture, the Resistance, the Sartre-Beauvoir relationship, the public break with Camus — this is philosophy as human drama. De Beauvoir's *Second Sex* is a natural bridge to feminist philosophy later. Camus's critique of Sartre (political commitment can become tyranny) is the internal tension.

### Week 19: Camus and the Absurd
**Mid-20th century — Algeria, Paris**
**The question:** If the universe is indifferent and meaning cannot be found, should you keep looking anyway — or live fully in spite of the silence?
**The break:** Unlike Sartre, Camus says you cannot create meaning — the world remains stubbornly absurd. But this is not a reason for despair. The absurd hero lives without appeal, without hope of resolution, and finds joy precisely in the refusal to stop pushing the rock.
**Key figures:** Camus, (Sisyphus as philosophical figure)
**Connection to previous:** The Sartre-Camus split of 1952 is one of the great intellectual dramas of the 20th century. Sartre said: commit to political action and create meaning through solidarity. Camus said: that road leads to the guillotine. *The Rebel* is his argument for revolt without revolution.
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 86–88, 224–228
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/camus/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong — but only if it's clearly distinguished from the existentialism entry. The key is Camus's biography (Algerian childhood, tuberculosis, the Nobel at 44, death at 46) and his specific disagreement with Sartre. If this feels like a rehash of Week 18, it fails. The distinction is precise: Sartre says create meaning; Camus says you can't, and that's okay.
**Note:** This entry was originally flagged as too thin for a standalone week. It works IF the Sartre-Camus split is treated as the central drama and Camus's Algerian identity (colonial subject, not Parisian intellectual) is foregrounded. If during research it still feels thin, merge into Week 18.

### Week 20: The Frankfurt School — The Culture Industry
**Mid-20th century — Frankfurt, then exile in New York and Los Angeles**
**The question:** What if the Enlightenment's promise of liberation through reason produced its opposite — new forms of domination?
**The break:** Reason itself can become an instrument of control. The "culture industry" (mass media, advertising, entertainment) doesn't just reflect society — it produces docile consumers who mistake consumption for freedom. Enlightenment, taken to its logical conclusion, becomes myth.
**Key figures:** Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse, (Benjamin)
**Connection to previous:** Camus diagnosed the absurd. The Frankfurt School asked: what if the absurd is not a cosmic condition but a manufactured one? What if the meaninglessness people feel is produced by the economic and cultural system they live in?
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 114 (Frankfurt School)
**SEP:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/adorno/
**Richness assessment:** Very strong and extremely relevant. The "culture industry" concept is immediately applicable to social media, streaming, algorithmic content. Adorno's famous line about poetry after Auschwitz is haunting. Benjamin's essay on the work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction is a salon classic. Risk: multiple thinkers, but they're a genuine school with shared premises.

## Arc 7: Unraveling and Reconstruction (Weeks 21+)

The remaining entries continue the story but are grouped here for future planning. They have not been through the same level of scrutiny as Weeks 1–20.

### Week 21: Structuralism
**1950s–1960s — Paris**
The individual is not the source of meaning. Language, culture, and social systems produce the subject, not the other way around.
**Key figures:** Saussure, Lévi-Strauss, Barthes
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 115–117

### Week 22: Foucault — Knowledge and Power
**1960s–1980s — Paris**
Knowledge is never neutral. Every claim to truth is entangled with power. Institutions (prisons, hospitals, schools) do not just reflect social norms — they produce them.
**Key figures:** Foucault
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 121–123

### Week 23: Derrida and Deconstruction
**1960s–1980s — Paris**
Language always undermines its own meanings. Every text contains contradictions that reveal what it tries to suppress. There is nothing outside the text.
**Key figures:** Derrida
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 119

### Week 24: Feminist Philosophy — De Beauvoir to Butler
**1949–present**
The "universal" subject of philosophy was always implicitly male. Feminist philosophy exposes and reconstructs the categories.
**Key figures:** De Beauvoir, Butler, hooks, Nussbaum
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 65, 89, 207
**Note:** De Beauvoir will already appear in Week 18. This entry should focus on *The Second Sex* as a founding text and then trace the development through second-wave feminism, intersectionality, and Butler's performativity. May need to be split into two entries depending on research depth.

### Week 25: Pragmatism
**Late 19th–early 20th century — America**
Truth is not a fixed correspondence between mind and world. Truth is what works — what helps us navigate experience and solve problems.
**Key figures:** James, Dewey, Peirce
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 84

### Week 26: Philosophy of Technology
**20th–21st century**
Technology is not a neutral tool. It reshapes perception, restructures social relations, and may be creating forms of life that existing moral frameworks cannot address.
**Key figures:** Heidegger (precursor), Byung-Chul Han, Zuboff
**Philosophize This:** Ep. 232, 235

## Sequencing Notes

**Why this order works:**
- Arc 1 (Ancient) gives the reader three rival answers to the same crisis. Accessible, dramatic, sets the pattern.
- Arc 2 (Faith/Reason) introduces the theological dimension that dominates Western thought for a millennium. Islamic philosophy is essential here — it's the transmission line that connects Aristotle to Aquinas.
- Arc 3 (Modern Turn) shows philosophy breaking free from theology. Machiavelli on politics, Descartes on knowledge, the empiricists on experience.
- Arc 4 (Revolutions) traces the political and moral consequences: social contract, Kantian duty, Hegelian history, utilitarian calculation.
- Arc 5 (Demolition) shows three thinkers tearing down what was built: Marx on economics, Nietzsche on morality, Kierkegaard on system-building itself.
- Arc 6 (Crisis of Meaning) follows the aftermath: phenomenology rebuilds from experience, existentialism confronts radical freedom, Camus refuses resolution, the Frankfurt School analyzes manufactured meaninglessness.
- Arc 7 (Unraveling) continues into structuralism, post-structuralism, feminism, pragmatism, and technology.

**Where the sequence can be paused:**
After Week 3, Week 6, Week 9, Week 13, Week 16, or Week 20. Each arc endpoint is a natural resting point.

**Entries flagged for possible merging:**
- Week 19 (Camus) into Week 18 (Existentialism) — if research shows they can't be clearly distinguished
- Week 22 (Foucault) and Week 23 (Derrida) — could potentially be combined into a single post-structuralism entry, but both are rich enough to stand alone

**Entries flagged for possible splitting:**
- Week 24 (Feminist Philosophy) — De Beauvoir to Butler may need two weeks
- Week 5 (Islamic Philosophy) — Avicenna and Averroes alone could each sustain an entry, but the school works as a unit
