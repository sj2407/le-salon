# Marx

For five years in the 1850s, Karl Marx lived with his family in two cramped rooms at 28 Dean Street, Soho, surviving on bread and potatoes, pawning his wife's silver, borrowing constantly from Friedrich Engels. Three of his six children died there before the age of nine. Most days he walked fifteen minutes to the British Museum Reading Room, where he sat among government factory reports, trade statistics, and parliamentary records, assembling the evidence for Das Kapital. The man writing the most comprehensive critique of capitalism could not afford a winter coat. What he saw in those reports convinced him that the misery in his own household was not misfortune but the inevitable cost built into the machine: a system that could not produce wealth without also producing deprivation.

He was writing in the thick of the first Industrial Revolution. In the textile mills of Manchester and Leeds, children as young as six worked fourteen-hour shifts. Entire families migrated from the countryside to factory cities where wages were low, housing was filthy, and life expectancy in working-class districts dropped below thirty. Engels had documented this firsthand in The Condition of the Working Class in England in 1844, cataloguing the lung disease, the mangled limbs, the rooms where ten people slept in shifts. Across Europe, the revolutions of 1848 had promised democratic reform and collapsed within months, crushed by the same ruling classes they had threatened. The liberal promise that free markets and constitutional government would improve everyone's condition was, for the factory worker coughing up cotton dust, visibly failing.

Marx had trained as a philosopher. His doctorate compared Democritus and Epicurus. But by his late twenties he had concluded that philosophy as it existed was asking the wrong question. Hegel had argued that history is the unfolding of Spirit through dialectical contradiction, each stage of thought generating its opposite until a higher synthesis emerges. Marx kept the dialectic but stripped out the Spirit. History does not move through the progress of ideas. It moves through changes in how human beings produce what they need to survive. The way a society organizes its labor, who owns the tools, who works them, and who takes the profit: these material conditions shape everything else. Law, religion, morality, philosophy itself are not independent discoveries of truth. They are what Marx called the superstructure, reflections of the economic base, generated by and serving the interests of whichever class controls production.

The engine driving this process forward is class struggle. In feudalism, lords extracted labor from serfs. In capitalism, the bourgeoisie extract what Marx called surplus value from workers who own nothing but their capacity to work. The worker produces more value than he is paid for; the difference is the owner's profit. What makes this more than an economic grievance is Marx's concept of alienation. The factory worker does not choose what to make, does not own what he produces, does not control how he spends his hours. He sells his labor as a commodity and in doing so becomes a commodity himself. Work, which Marx believed should be the fullest expression of human creativity, becomes instead the thing that empties a person out.

This is where Marx broke from the utilitarians who preceded him. Bentham and Mill asked how to distribute happiness more efficiently across a population. Marx asked a prior question: who built the capitalist order that distributes it, and whose interests does that order serve? For Marx, you cannot reform capitalism from within because capitalism generates the moral vocabulary that makes it look fair. When a worker who owns nothing signs a contract with an employer who owns everything, the language of free exchange disguises what is actually extraction. The ruling class does not simply hold power; it produces the concepts of merit, opportunity, and fairness that make its power appear natural and pre-ordained. The alternative Marx proposed was not gradual reform but revolution: the working class seizing control of the means of production and abolishing the class system that requires one group to sell its labor to another.

Karl Popper, writing a century later, saw in all of this a pattern he recognized from other systems of thought that claim to explain everything. Marx had predicted that capitalism would drive wages down relentlessly, that the middle class would disappear into the proletariat, and that revolution would erupt first in the most industrialized nations, where the contradictions between owners and workers were sharpest. None of this happened as described. Real wages in industrialized countries rose over the following decades. Trade unions won shorter hours, safer conditions, and legal protections. A substantial middle class expanded rather than vanished. And when revolution came, it came in 1917 to Russia, a largely agrarian empire, not to industrial England or Germany. Popper's charge was that Marxists responded to each of these disconfirmations not by revising the theory but by reinterpreting it: the revolution was delayed, not disproven; the reforms were temporary concessions, not real change. A scientific theory, Popper argued, must specify in advance what evidence would prove it wrong. If no possible outcome can count against it, the theory is not describing the world; it is telling a story elastic enough to absorb any fact. Defenders of Marx counter that the labor reforms which softened capitalism were won by movements that drew their diagnosis and their demands from Marx's own writings. The theory did not fail; it changed the conditions it described. Whether that defense rescues Marx or merely proves Popper's point about a theory that cannot lose is itself an unresolved question.

You wake up on Monday and spend the next five days producing work you did not choose, for purposes you do not set, under artificial deadlines attached to objectives three levels of management above you. You optimize metrics that exist to justify someone else's reporting structure. The urgency is manufactured. Nobody's life depends on it. By Friday at six you start to feel like yourself again, and by Sunday evening the dread is already settling back in. Marx would say the numbness you feel is not a personal failing but a structural feature of a system designed to extract your productive energy and return as little as it can get away with. And the diagnosis rings true. But every attempt to build the alternative Marx prescribed, collective ownership, democratic control of production, has so far produced new forms of unfreedom without eliminating the old ones. Does that knowledge liberate you, because you finally see that this is not a personal failing but a structural one, or does it shackle you, because there is no known alternative?
