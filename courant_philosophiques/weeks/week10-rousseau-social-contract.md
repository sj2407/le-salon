# Rousseau and the Social Contract

In the autumn of 1749, Jean-Jacques Rousseau was walking from Paris to the prison at Vincennes to visit his friend Denis Diderot. He was thirty-seven, a self-taught dropout who had converted from Calvinism to Catholicism, wandered through apprenticeships, and left Geneva at sixteen with no plan for what came next. Reading a newspaper on the road, he came across an essay competition from the Academy of Dijon: "Has the restoration of the sciences and arts contributed to the purification of morals?" Rousseau later described what happened as an illumination. He sat down under a tree and wept. The insight that overtook him would drive the rest of his life: human beings are naturally good, and civilization has corrupted them.

He won the prize and became famous overnight. But the fuller thesis came in the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755), where Rousseau traced the corruption to its source. In the state of nature, humans lived as solitary creatures guided by two instincts: self-preservation (*amour de soi*) and pity for the suffering of others (*pitié*). The trouble began when people started living together. Proximity bred a new form of self-regard Rousseau called *amour propre*: not simply pride, but a pride that depends entirely on how you rank against others. A person driven by *amour propre* does not ask "do I have enough?" but "do I have more than my neighbor?" That question made accumulation a measure of worth. Someone planted a fence and declared "this is mine," and others were foolish enough to believe him. Property became a new line between the haves and the have-nots, enforced by laws written to protect what the rich had taken. Civilization was not progress. It was a swindle dressed as order.

Voltaire, who received a copy, sent a reply that has become famous: "Never has so much wit been used in the design of making us stupid. One is seized with a desire to walk on all fours when reading your work." But Rousseau was not proposing a return to the forest. The state of nature was a thought experiment, not a destination. The question was whether a legitimate political order could be built from the wreckage.

His answer was *The Social Contract* (1762). "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." No one has a natural right to rule over others. The only legitimate basis for power is an agreement among free and equal persons to form a community governed by the *volonté générale*, the general will: what the community would choose if every citizen set aside private advantage and asked only what serves the common good. Citizens who obey it are not submitting to an external authority. They are obeying a law they gave themselves.

The difficulty is what happens to those who disagree. Rousseau's answer is the sentence that has haunted his legacy: anyone who refuses to obey the general will shall be, in Rousseau's phrase, "forced to be free." He meant that someone who defects from the agreement while enjoying its benefits is a free rider, and the community has the right to compel compliance. But if the general will is always right by definition, and dissenters can be coerced in its name, then the machinery can be coopted by any faction that claims to speak for the people to silence those who resist.

This is exactly what happened. In 1789 the French Revolution erupted. Rousseau had been dead for eleven years, but Robespierre carried *The Social Contract* as a personal talisman and invoked the general will to justify the Reign of Terror. Benjamin Constant, writing in the aftermath, identified the structural problem. Rousseau had confused what Constant called *la liberté des anciens* with *la liberté des modernes*, the liberty of the ancients with the liberty of the moderns. Ancient liberty was collective: citizens in Athens or Sparta voted on laws and wars together, but the state regulated religion, marriage, dress, and speech. Nothing was private. Modern liberty is the reverse: it protects what no authority can touch, the individual's beliefs, associations, and right to be left alone. Rousseau's general will, by granting the collective unlimited authority over the individual, had imported the ancient model into a world built on individual rights.

The logic did not stop with Robespierre. Rousseau's general will requires a unified "people" whose common interest can be discovered. But in practice, the question is always who counts as the people. In apartheid South Africa, a white minority ran democratic institutions among themselves and excluded the Black majority from the franchise entirely. The general will was real enough for those inside it. For everyone else, it was a locked door with a sign that read "freedom." In the United States, the Senate grants Wyoming the same representation as California, and a president can take office without winning the popular vote. Hitler came to power through elections and invoked the Volk with the same confidence Robespierre invoked the nation. The flaw is not that majorities always crush minorities. It is that the general will, once declared, can define who belongs and who does not, and those left outside the definition have no recourse within the system that excluded them.

Rousseau would say you are bound by the social contract because freedom means living under laws you helped create, even when you lost the vote. Constant would say no process, however legitimate, can authorize the violation of individual rights, and that a people who strip a minority of its liberty are still tyrants regardless of how they voted. The question is what you believe when these two principles collide: whether a democratic process that produces an unjust result is still binding, or whether there are things no collective, no matter how large or how legitimate, has the right to do to the people inside it.
