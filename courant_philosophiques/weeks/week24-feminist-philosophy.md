# Feminist Philosophy — De Beauvoir to Butler

In 1946, sitting at the Café des Deux Magots in Paris, Simone de Beauvoir set out to write a short autobiographical essay. She intended to describe herself, and found she could not begin without answering a prior question: what has it meant to be a woman? Her own immediate response was that the question hardly applied. She had passed the agrégation in philosophy at twenty-one, finishing second to Sartre. She lived independently. Then she looked again at the world she had moved through, at its books, its laws, its assumptions, and saw that this world had always been a masculine one, and that her own freedom inside it had been the privilege of someone permitted to forget her sex. *The Second Sex* came out three years later.

The book opens with a structural claim. Humanity, Beauvoir wrote, has defined itself as masculine. Woman is not defined in herself but only in relation to man, as his other. Drawing on Hegel's account of master and slave, she called this position the Inessential Other: the figure whose meaning is always given by the term she stands against. Drawing on Sartre, she argued that the position blocks what existentialism took to be the structure of human life, transcendence. By transcendence Beauvoir meant the movement of a free subject toward future projects, the work of shaping the world rather than being shaped by it. Its opposite she called immanence: the state of being held inside a fixed role, repeated without choice. Patriarchy was the social machinery that condemned women to immanence and reserved transcendence for men. This is the argument under the famous line: "On ne naît pas femme: on le devient." One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman. Anatomy is one fact among many. Woman as a social kind is produced by education, custom, and law, and what has been produced can in principle be unmade. The Vatican placed it on the Index of Prohibited Books. Camus said she had made the French male look ridiculous. It sold twenty thousand copies in its first week.

Within a generation, Beauvoir's woman had a problem. In *Ain't I a Woman: Black Women and Feminism*, drafted at nineteen and published in 1981, bell hooks asked which women second-wave feminism had been speaking about. Beauvoir had described women as confined to the home, denied access to public work, alienated from their bodies, kept economically dependent. That description fit a particular woman, hooks pointed out: white, middle-class, married, kept out of the labor market by the very social position that defined her oppression. Black women in the United States had spent centuries doing the labor that allowed white women the leisure to feel oppressed by its absence. They had been sexually objectified through the inverse of pure femininity, the myth of permanent availability that justified the rape of enslaved women and the unpunished rape of their descendants. The title hooks took from Sojourner Truth's speech at the 1851 Akron Women's Rights Convention put the point in a line. When white feminism's "woman" took the housewife as its representative figure and called for her liberation into public work, it took one particular history and named it universal.

Nine years later, in *Gender Trouble*, Judith Butler escalated the problem. Where hooks had argued that the standard definition of woman excluded most women, Butler argued that any definition would exclude. To define is to set conditions for membership, and any conditions exclude those who fail to meet them, which is how hooks had been able to find Beauvoir's woman wanting in the first place. Butler's conclusion: feminism had been mistaken in its starting assumption. Gender was not a stable identity that politics could represent. It was performative, instituted through "a stylized repetition of acts": the walk, the voice, the gesture, the way of sitting, the cut of the clothes, repeated so often that they appeared to express an inner essence rather than produce one. The drag performer, inhabiting femininity self-consciously, exposed the trick. There was no original being copied, only the copy. From this followed a politics whose aim was disrupting the rituals through which the category was held in place rather than representing women. The task was to make the apparatus stutter.

The sharpest attack on this came from inside feminism. In February 1999, Martha Nussbaum, a professor of law and philosophy at the University of Chicago, published "The Professor of Parody" in *The New Republic*. Nussbaum is the political theorist most associated with the capabilities approach, which evaluates a society by what concrete activities it permits its members to undertake: reading, voting, walking safely, owning property, refusing a marriage, raising a child without fear of beating. By that measure, she wrote, Butler had abandoned the project. Women raped without legal recourse, denied literacy, beaten by their husbands without protection from courts, did not need parodic performances of gender. They needed laws. They needed schools that admitted girls, ordinances that protected workers, prosecutors who would charge rape inside marriage. Butler's politics, Nussbaum argued, had retreated into the seminar room and called the retreat radical. "For women who are hungry, illiterate, disenfranchised, beaten, raped," she wrote, "it is not sexy or liberating to reenact, however parodically, the conditions of hunger, illiteracy, disenfranchisement, beating, and rape. Such women prefer food, schools, votes, and the integrity of their bodies." She closed with the sentence that became the most quoted line of the controversy: "Judith Butler's hip quietism is a comprehensible response to the difficulty of realizing justice in America. But it is a bad response. It collaborates with evil. Feminism demands more and women deserve better."

When you speak in the name of women, when you say women's rights or women's experience or women's bodies, whose body are you thinking of, and whose are you leaving out? And once you have an honest answer, do you stop defining the category, or do you keep defining it and fight for the laws and schools and prosecutions you cannot fight for in a category's absence?
