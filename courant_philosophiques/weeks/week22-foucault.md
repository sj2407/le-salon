# Foucault: Knowledge and Power

On 28 March 1757, Robert-François Damiens was taken in a cart to the Place de Grève in Paris. He had tried to stab Louis XV in January and been convicted of regicide, which the law punished as parricide, since the king was held to be the father of the realm. Over several hours, before tens of thousands of spectators, executioners tore flesh from his arms and thighs with red-hot pincers, poured molten lead and burning sulphur into the wounds, and harnessed four horses to his limbs to rend the body apart. Damiens was the last person drawn and quartered in France.

Michel Foucault opens *Discipline and Punish* (1975) with this scene, then turns the page to Léon Faucher's 1838 rulebook for the House of Young Prisoners in Paris. Wake at 6. Prayer at 6:05. Labor at 6:30. Rest at 8:25. Every minute of the inmate's day is accounted for, and no one is touched. The argument of the book rests on what separates those two pages. The decades between are usually told as a story of humanization, in which Enlightenment reason drove torture out of European life. Foucault rejects this reading. Between the two pages a new technology of power appears. Foucault uses the phrase to name a coordinated set of techniques and arrangements for shaping behavior. Instead of punishing the body, it trains it. He wrote the book in the early 1970s, in post-1968 Paris, after co-founding the Groupe d'information sur les prisons to gather testimony from inmates, and while lecturing at the Collège de France on the history of power.

To see how Foucault arrived at this argument, look at the structuralism he was leaving behind. Structuralism, the dominant French intellectual movement of the 1950s and 60s, applied the methods of structural linguistics to literature, anthropology, and psychoanalysis. Its core claim was that the individual does not invent meaning. Larger systems like language, kinship rules, and social categories precede any individual and shape what that person can think and say. Each person is the product of their culture and environment, not its source. Foucault accepts this claim. What he rejects is the structuralist method, which theorized these systems as static, abstract patterns at a single point in time, and could not explain how they came into being or changed over time.

To address this blindspot, Foucault turns to Nietzsche, who in the 1880s developed a method he called *genealogy*. A genealogy in this context is a historical investigation. It takes a present-day concept or value, the way Nietzsche took morality and Foucault took madness, criminality, sexuality, and traces it back to the specific historical struggles that produced it, asking who fought over the category, who won, and what the win consolidated. The genealogist treats categories like 'sanity' or 'normality' as the products of contested historical processes that could have had different outcomes. Foucault uses the word *contingent* to mark this: nothing about the present was inevitable.

Whereas the structuralists studied language as their primary system, Foucault studied institutions: the asylum, the clinic, the confessional, the prison, the school, the factory, where people get classified, measured, and treated, and where what counts as sanity, health, honesty, criminality, ability, and productivity gets decided.

The central thesis is that knowledge and power cannot be separated. Institutions like prisons and clinics not only exercise authority over people but also generate detailed knowledge about them: files, examinations, classifications. That knowledge is itself a form of authority.

Foucault contrasts this modern arrangement with what he calls *sovereign power*, the older form that put Damiens on the scaffold. Sovereign power operates by subtraction: it takes life, wealth, time. *Disciplinary power*, the kind encoded in Faucher's schedule, operates by producing norms and selves. It generates categories of person, like 'the criminal' or 'the homosexual'. It generates standards of conduct, like what a good student or worker looks like. And it shapes the inner sense each person has of who they are. This is what Foucault means when he calls modern power *productive*: power's capacity to bring categories, norms, and selves into being through institutional action.

He names three techniques at the core of disciplinary power. The first, *hierarchical observation*, means arranging space and lines of sight so that some people can continuously watch others while remaining out of view themselves: the guard tower, the teacher's raised desk, the open-plan office, the security camera. The second, *normalizing judgment*, ranks each person against a norm. Normalizing here is the act of measuring against a standard, placing each person on a continuous scale of more-or-less-normal. An idea of the good prisoner, student, worker, or citizen serves as the benchmark. Where the law uses a binary of permitted and forbidden, disciplinary institutions work in continuous degrees. The third, *the examination*, combines the first two: a school test, a medical check-up, an annual review records where each person stands on the scale, accumulates the record into a file, and hands back an identity the institution has produced.

The English philosopher Jeremy Bentham, writing in the late 18th century, designed an architectural plan he called the *panopticon* that gives this arrangement physical form. It is a ring of backlit cells around a central tower from which a single guard can see any inmate but no inmate can see the guard. The arrangement works through uncertainty. The inmate, never sure whether he is being watched at any given moment, comes to act as if he always might be. He monitors himself. Foucault's phrase for this is that the inmate becomes "the principle of his own subjection": power no longer needs an external enforcer because the inmate has interiorized it. The diagram extends beyond prisons. It is the form of the factory floor, the classroom, the hospital ward, and the performance review.

*The History of Sexuality, Volume 1* (1976) brings the same method to the modern story about sexuality. The familiar version of that story is one of repression and liberation: the Victorians silenced sex, and modernity, with Freud and the sexual revolution, set it free. Foucault tested the claim by counting what was actually said about sex over the period in question. He found that talk about sex had multiplied. The confessional, the medical examination, the psychiatric interview, the demographic survey, the school questionnaire: each became a place where people were required to put their desires into words. The doctor and the psychiatrist took over the role the priest once held.

Foucault's argument is that this proliferation of talk produced modern sexuality. Through repeated confession, classification, and diagnosis, 'sexuality' came into being as a category of identity. So did the kinds of people the category sorts: the homosexual, the hysteric, the pervert, the normal. People came to police their own desires against those categories. The story of repression and liberation gets the causal direction wrong. What looked like the path to liberation was the apparatus that brought the categories of sexual identity into being in the first place.

The most influential criticism comes from Jürgen Habermas, in *The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity* (1985). Foucault wants his reader to be outraged by the prison, the clinic, the asylum, to see them as machineries of domination. But outrage needs a standard the institution is violating: freedom, dignity, the autonomy of the person being processed. Foucault's own theory says every such standard is itself a product of power. So where does the outrage come from? He relies on values his account denies exist. Habermas calls this *cryptonormativism*: the smuggling in of evaluative claims by a theory that officially refuses them. Nancy Fraser puts the point more plainly. Foucault, she writes, offers "empirical insights and normative confusions." He sees the institutions accurately. He cannot tell us why we should mind.

Daniel Zamora raises a different objection. Foucault's 1978–79 lectures at the Collège de France, published as *The Birth of Biopolitics*, take up neoliberalism: the doctrine that elevates markets, competition, and the entrepreneurial individual over state planning and welfare provision. Foucault treats this doctrine with something close to sympathy, as a possible escape from the disciplinary and welfare state he had spent a decade criticizing. But neoliberalism, Zamora argues, is itself a regime of normalization. It produces the entrepreneurial subject the way the prison produced the delinquent, replacing the timetable with the performance score and the resume. The method Foucault built to expose disciplinary power should have been turned on the new arrangement. It was not.

Think about how you describe yourself now. Maybe through a diagnosis: ADHD, depression, high-functioning anxious. Maybe through a workplace ranking: senior, junior, on a performance improvement plan. Maybe through an app: your steps, your sleep score, your match rate. Maybe through a personality test: an Enneagram type, an MBTI letter, an attachment style. None of these categories existed as ways of understanding a person a century ago. Some did not exist twenty years ago. Each was produced somewhere, by a clinical study, a workplace metric, a marketing apparatus, an algorithm, and handed back to you as a description of who you are. You did not invent them. They have come to organize your sense of self. This is what Foucault means when he says modern power is productive. Its central work is making you into the kind of person it can measure.

What Foucault wants you to notice is the relief. The moment a doctor names what you have been struggling with, something gives way. There is finally a word for it, a population that shares it, a protocol that addresses it. The feeling of inadequacy you have carried for years becomes a condition with a literature. Is that the relief of finally seeing yourself clearly? Or is the inadequacy itself produced because the category existed to catch it, manufactured by the standard you were already being measured against? Foucault never resolved this. The line between recognition and capture is the question his readers are still inside.
