# Scholasticism and Aquinas

His classmates in Cologne called him the Dumb Ox. Thomas was tall, heavy, and silent. He sat through lectures and disputations without speaking, and the other Dominican students took his quiet for stupidity. His teacher, Albert the Great, saw something else. "You call him a Dumb Ox," Albert said, "but his bellowing will one day fill the world." Within two decades, Thomas Aquinas would produce the most ambitious synthesis of faith and reason the Christian world had ever attempted. Then, on December 6, 1273, while celebrating Mass in Naples, he stopped writing and never resumed.

Aquinas arrived at the University of Paris in the 1250s at a moment of intellectual crisis. For centuries, Christian theology had operated within an Augustinian framework: the soul turns inward, finds God's traces, and depends on grace and scripture for knowledge of the divine. Philosophy served theology, and Plato provided its vocabulary. But in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, the full corpus of Aristotle's works arrived in Latin translation, most of it filtered through the commentaries of the Islamic philosopher Averroes. Aristotle offered something Augustine never had: a complete system of logic, physics, metaphysics, and ethics built entirely on reason and observation, with no reference to revelation. The conservative theologians wanted Aristotle banned. The radical Aristotelians wanted to pursue philosophy wherever it led, even when its conclusions contradicted Church teaching. In 1270, the Bishop of Paris condemned thirteen propositions drawn from Aristotelian philosophy, including the claim that the world is eternal.

Aquinas rejected both positions. Augustine had treated reason as useful but subordinate. Averroes had argued that philosophy can reach the same truths as revelation on its own. Al-Ghazali had argued the opposite, that philosophy is the wrong instrument entirely. Aquinas carved out a third path: faith and reason cannot genuinely conflict because both originate in God. Where they overlap, on questions like whether God exists or whether the soul is immortal, reason can reach the same conclusions that faith affirms. But reason has a ceiling. The Trinity, the Incarnation, the specifics of salvation exceed its natural range the way sunlight exceeds the capacity of a bat's eye. These require revelation. Aquinas called the truths accessible to both faith and reason the "preambles of faith," and the Five Ways are his demonstration that reason can reach them.

In the opening questions of the Summa Theologiae, Aquinas lays out five arguments for God's existence, each beginning from something observable. Things move; therefore there must be a first mover that is itself unmoved. Effects have causes; therefore there must be a first cause that is itself uncaused. Contingent beings exist and cease to exist; therefore there must be a necessary being whose existence depends on nothing else. Things exhibit degrees of perfection; therefore there must be a being of maximal perfection. Natural bodies act toward ends without possessing knowledge; therefore there must be an intelligence directing them. Each argument follows the same structure: start with what the senses can verify, reason backward through a chain that cannot extend to infinity, and arrive at something the chain requires but cannot contain.

William of Ockham, writing half a century after Aquinas's death, applied his razor to the entire synthesis. Where Aquinas emphasized God's rationality and the intelligibility of creation, Ockham emphasized God's radical freedom. If God is truly omnipotent, Ockham argued, he could have created the world according to entirely different principles. The order we observe is contingent on God's will, not necessary to his nature. Reason cannot work backward from the world to God, because the world could have been otherwise. God's existence, Ockham concluded, is held by faith alone. He did not consider this a defeat. He considered it honest: the finite mind should not pretend to have measured the infinite.

Aquinas never finished the Summa. On that December morning in 1273, after whatever he experienced during Mass, he told his secretary Reginald: "I can write no more. All that I have written seems to me like straw." He died three months later. The Aquinas-Ockham divide has never been settled, and you do not need to be a theologian to feel it. When you hold a conviction about how to live, about what is right, about what matters, do you believe that conviction rests on something your mind can actually reach, some structure in reality that careful thinking can uncover? Or is it, at bottom, a commitment you have made that no argument could fully justify, and that honesty requires you to hold without pretending reason put it there?
