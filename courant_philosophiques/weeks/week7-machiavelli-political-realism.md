# Machiavelli and Political Realism

In February 1513, Niccolò Machiavelli was hauled into the Bargello prison and given six drops of the strappado, a torture that hoists the body by bound arms until the shoulders dislocate. He had served Florence's republic for fourteen years as diplomat and military organizer. When the Medici family overthrew the republic in 1512 and reclaimed the city, Machiavelli was dismissed from office. Months later his name surfaced on a list of conspirators plotting against the new Medici regime. He never confessed. Released under a papal amnesty, he was exiled to a small farm, forbidden from political life.

He had spent his career studying how power is won and lost. Now, having lost it himself, he wrote with the clarity of someone who had no illusions left. Each evening on the farm he shed his mud-caked clothes and dressed in court garments to read ancient historians for hours. By December he had composed a short treatise called *De Principatibus*. We know it as *The Prince*.

The book attacks two thousand years of political thought. In Chapter 15, Machiavelli announces he will pursue the "effectual truth of the thing" rather than "the imagination of it." Many have imagined republics and principalities that never existed, he writes. The targets are unmistakable: Plato's philosopher-kings ruling through knowledge of the Good, Augustine's City of God, Aquinas's argument that political law derives from divine reason. Different thinkers, different arguments, but Machiavelli dismisses them all for the same failing: they describe how rulers should live rather than how rulers do live. A man who tries to be good among so many who are not good will be destroyed.

He redefines *virtù*, which his predecessors used to mean moral excellence, as effectiveness: the capacity to read circumstances and act decisively, whether mercifully or cruelly. Fortune controls roughly half of human affairs. The other half belongs to whoever possesses virtù enough to master it. A prince must be both fox and lion: cunning enough to recognize traps, strong enough to frighten wolves. He must appear merciful, faithful, and religious while being prepared to act against every one of those qualities when necessity demands.

The unsettling core of the argument is not that rulers do terrible things. Everyone knew that. It is that Machiavelli refuses to call those things sins. Where Aquinas would say a ruler who deceives has failed morally even if he succeeded politically, Machiavelli says the deception was virtù if it preserved the state. The vocabulary of sin and redemption drops away entirely.

Whether he meant it is genuinely unclear. The *Discourses on Livy*, written alongside *The Prince*, praises republican government and popular liberty. Rousseau argued in *The Social Contract* that Machiavelli was "an honest man and a good citizen" who veiled his love of liberty, and that the contradiction between *The Prince* and the *Discourses* proves he has been read only by "superficial or corrupt readers." On this reading, *The Prince* is not a manual for tyrants but an exposure of how tyranny works, dedicated to the Medici by a man who needed their favor to survive. The debate has lasted five centuries because the text supports both readings.

Machiavelli never said morality is irrelevant. He wrote that a prince "should not deviate from what is good, if that is possible, but he should know how to do evil, if that is necessary." The real question is who decides when necessity has arrived. Once effectiveness replaces goodness as the measure of political action, "necessity" can stretch to cover almost anything. Every leader who has ever broken a promise or crushed a rival has claimed the situation demanded it. Machiavelli gives no test for distinguishing genuine necessity from convenient rationalization, and that silence is where his argument is most vulnerable.

You vote for a candidate whose record includes things you find indefensible because the alternative is worse. You work for an institution that serves most of the people it touches and damages some of them, and you stay because leaving would not undo the damage and would remove the help you provide. You make a deal with someone whose values offend you because the project cannot move forward without them. Each time, you tell yourself necessity required it. Machiavelli would say you are seeing clearly. But are you? How many times can that reasoning win before it stops being a clear-eyed judgment and becomes a rationalization that lets you avoid looking at who bore the cost of your pragmatism?

**Defining Quote**

"I have thought it proper to represent things as they are in real truth, rather than as they are imagined. Many have dreamed up republics and principalities which have never in truth been known to exist; the gulf between how one should live and how one does live is so wide that a man who neglects what is actually done for what should be done learns the way to self-destruction rather than self-preservation."
— *The Prince*, Chapter 15 (trans. George Bull)

**Reading List**

- *The Prince* (Niccolò Machiavelli, 1513): The treatise that severed politics from theology. Best in the Harvey Mansfield translation for precision, or the George Bull translation for readability.
- *Discourses on Livy* (Niccolò Machiavelli, c. 1517): The companion work revealing Machiavelli's republican sympathies, essential for understanding whether *The Prince* is the whole story.
- *Machiavelli: A Very Short Introduction* (Quentin Skinner, 2000): The best brief scholarly overview, situating Machiavelli in his Florentine context.
