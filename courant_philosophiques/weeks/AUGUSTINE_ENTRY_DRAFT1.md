# Augustine and Early Christian Philosophy

In 386, a thirty-one-year-old professor of rhetoric sat in a garden in Milan, weeping under a fig tree. He had spent nine years among the Manicheans, a dualist sect, before Bishop Ambrose convinced him that Christianity could be taken seriously by an educated mind. He understood all of this intellectually. He could not act on it. His will was split: one half reaching toward a life of ascetic devotion, the other held fast by the habits he had built over a decade. Then he heard a child's voice beyond the garden wall, chanting: tolle lege, tolle lege. Take up and read. He opened Paul's epistle to the Romans at random and landed on a passage about putting on Christ and making no provision for the flesh. The war in him stopped.

Augustine told that scene, from Book 8 of the Confessions, to demonstrate something he believed was true about every human being: the will is broken. You can know the good, see it clearly, want it sincerely, and still find yourself unable to do it. This was his direct challenge to the ancient philosophical tradition he had inherited. For the Greeks, the problem of wrongdoing was a problem of knowledge. Socrates argued that no one does evil willingly; if you truly understood the good, you would pursue it. Augustine thought this was obviously false. He had stolen pears as a teenager, not because he wanted pears, not because he was hungry, but because the act of taking what was forbidden pleased him. He devoted several pages of the Confessions to this petty theft, not out of scrupulousness but because the episode exposed something the Greek account could not explain: a will that desires its own defiance.

The philosophical term Augustine developed for this condition was original sin, though the concept is more precise than the label. He did not mean that humans are born wicked. He meant that the will arrives damaged, inclined toward lesser goods at the expense of greater ones, drawn to the immediate over the lasting. Left to its own resources, the will cannot repair itself. This is why Augustine's most provocative prayer infuriated his critics: "Give what you command, and command what you will." If you must ask God for the power to obey God, obedience is not something you earn. It is something you receive.

Pelagius, a British monk living in Rome, heard Augustine's prayer and recoiled. He argued that God would not command what humans could not perform. Adam's sin affected Adam alone. Every person is born with a will capable of choosing the good without divine assistance. Grace might help, but it is not necessary. Augustine's position prevailed at the Council of Ephesus in 431, the year after his death, and Pelagius was condemned as a heretic. But the question he raised has never been retired. He did not deny that people do wrong. He denied that they must. For Pelagius, wrongdoing is always a failure of resolve, never a condition of nature.

Augustine's answer carries a further cost. If human beings cannot repair themselves, they depend on grace. And if grace is given by God to some and withheld from others, predestination follows. His later writings pushed toward this conclusion, and it troubled even his allies: God chooses whom to save, and the rest are justly condemned. The monk Cassian and others in southern Gaul pushed back, arguing that grace and human effort must cooperate, that the will is wounded but not dead. This position was condemned at the Council of Orange in 529, but the discomfort it expressed has never gone away. How much of your moral life belongs to you? When you act justly, is that your doing, or something working through you that you did not earn and cannot summon again at will? And when you do wrong willfully, is the honest response to redouble your effort or to admit that effort was never sufficient?
