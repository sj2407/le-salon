# Kant and the Moral Law

Immanuel Kant never left Königsberg. Born there in 1724, he studied, taught, and died in the same Prussian city, scarcely five feet tall, with a deformed chest and a weak constitution that he managed through maniacal discipline. He rose at five, lectured, wrote, and walked every afternoon at exactly the same time along the same route. His neighbors set their clocks by him. He missed his walk only once, the day he received Rousseau's *Émile* and could not put it down. The only portrait in his study was of Rousseau.

The portrait was revealing. Rousseau had argued that ordinary people possess a moral dignity that philosophers had ignored. Kant took this seriously enough to rethink his teachings. He had been trained in rationalist metaphysics, confident that reason could prove the existence of God, the immortality of the soul, and the structure of reality. Then he read Hume. Hume had argued that what we call causation is nothing more than the habit of expecting one event to follow another; we never observe a necessary connection, only sequence. Hume was not attacking science. His target was the metaphysician's claim to know *why* nature behaves as it does. Once that claim fell, the rationalist project of proving God, the soul, and the moral order through pure reason fell with it. Kant later wrote that Hume had woken him from his "dogmatic slumber."

He spent the next decade in near silence. When he emerged in 1781, the result was the *Critique of Pure Reason*, an 800-page argument that accepted Hume's challenge and answered it. Hume was right that we never observe causation directly. But Kant argued this is because causation is not something we find in the world; it is something the mind imposes on experience. We cannot perceive anything without placing it in space, in time, and in a chain of cause and effect. We know the world only as it appears once the mind has organized it. The world as it is before the mind gets to it, what Kant called the noumenal world, remains forever out of reach.

This destroyed traditional metaphysics, which had claimed to prove God's existence and the soul's immortality through reason alone. But Kant argued the limitation opened a door. If reason cannot reach God, freedom, or immortality as objects of knowledge, it can reach them through a different route: morality. "I had to deny knowledge," he wrote, "in order to make room for faith."

The *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785) laid out the argument. Nothing in the world, Kant claimed, can be called unconditionally good except a good will: the resolve to act solely out of duty, regardless of praise or reward. Only the will that acts from duty is good no matter the circumstances. But what does duty actually require? Kant's answer was the categorical imperative: before you act, ask whether you would want to live in a world where everyone acts on the same principle. If the answer is no, the action is wrong. Take lying. You might benefit from a single lie, but you cannot wish for a world where everyone lies and trust disappears. The test is not whether the action helps you in this case. The test is whether the principle behind it survives being applied to everyone.

Kant added a second test: never use another person as merely a means to get what you want. Every rational being has their own goals, their own judgment, their own life to lead. To manipulate or deceive someone is to act as if their interests are secondary to yours. Things have a price; people have dignity.

Benjamin Constant, the same thinker who challenged Rousseau's general will, put Kant's principle to its most famous test. Suppose a murderer comes to your door and asks whether your friend is hiding inside. Kant, Constant charged, would require you to tell the truth even if it costs your friend's life. Kant replied in 1797 with "On a Supposed Right to Lie from Philanthropy," and he did not flinch. A lie, even one told to save a life, wrongs humanity because it cracks the foundation of all agreements, contracts, and trust between persons. If you tell the truth and your friend is murdered, the crime is entirely the murderer's responsibility. If you lie and your friend, who had slipped out the back, is found by the murderer on the street, you share the blame.

The example has horrified readers for two centuries. After the Second World War, it became unavoidable: should those hiding Jews from the Nazis have told the truth at the door? Kant's answer appears to be yes, and this is the fracture at the center of his system. A morality built entirely on principle, with no room for consequences, can demand actions whose results are monstrous. Kant would retort that consequences are outside your control. If morality depends on outcomes, then whether you are a moral person depends on things you cannot predict or guarantee. The only thing entirely within your power is whether you acted from duty. That answer means nothing to the friend behind the door.

A close friend asks what you honestly think of the person they are about to marry. You have reservations. Kant says you owe them the truth: lying, even to spare their feelings, denies them the chance to choose for themselves. But sometimes people ask for honesty when all they want is reassurance. You tell the truth and the friendship ends. The wedding happens anyway. Did you do the right thing?

> "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."
> — Immanuel Kant, *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785)
