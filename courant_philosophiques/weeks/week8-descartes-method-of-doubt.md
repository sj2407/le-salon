# Descartes and the Method of Doubt

On the night of November 10, 1619, a twenty-three-year-old French soldier named René Descartes sat alone in a stove-heated room in a small Bavarian town near the Danube. He was wintering with the army of the Duke of Bavaria, with no battles to fight and no company to keep. For days he had been turning over a single frustration: everything he had learned at the Jesuit college of La Flèche amounted to a heap of disputed claims and competing authorities. Mathematics alone offered certainty. That night, feverish with concentration, he had three dreams. Phantoms chased him through streets. A thunderclap filled his room with sparks. A book of poetry fell open to a single line: what path shall I take in life? He took the dreams as a sign. His path would be to find a method that could give all knowledge the certainty of geometry.

It took him eighteen years to act on the vision, and even then, fear dictated the form. By 1633 he had completed a treatise on physics, *The World*, that explained nature through matter and motion alone. But that year the Roman Inquisition condemned Galileo for defending the view that the Earth moves around the Sun. Descartes, a Catholic who had spent his adult life in the Protestant Netherlands precisely to work undisturbed, shelved the manuscript. "It is not my temperament to set sail against the wind," he wrote to a friend. Unable to publish his physics, he turned to a different project entirely. The *Discourse on the Method*, published in 1637, was not a physics textbook but a philosophical autobiography, written in French rather than Latin so that anyone with reason could follow the argument.

The argument begins with a demolition. Suppose your senses deceive you: a straight stick looks bent in water, a distant tower looks round when it is square. Suppose you are dreaming and there is no world at all. Suppose a malicious demon is feeding you illusions, making you believe that two plus three equals five when it does not. What survives? One thing: the act of doubting itself cannot be doubted, because to doubt is to think, and to think is to exist. *Cogito ergo sum*. From this single certainty Descartes set out to rebuild everything. Among my ideas is the idea of a perfect being, which could not have originated in my imperfect mind, so God must exist. And because God is not a deceiver, the clear and distinct perceptions he has given me can be trusted. The external world, cautiously, is restored.

What Descartes left behind was a fracture that no one has repaired. If the mind is a thinking substance with no spatial dimensions and the body is an extended substance that occupies space, how do the two interact? Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia, a twenty-four-year-old exile living in The Hague, posed this question in a letter in May 1643. How can the soul, "it being only a thinking substance," determine the body's movements to bring about voluntary actions? For the mind to move the body it would need to make contact, and contact requires being somewhere. But Descartes had defined the mind as having no location at all. His replies grew evasive. He admitted that the union of mind and body was something grasped only through "ordinary life and conversation," not through the intellect. Elisabeth was unconvinced. She told him it would be easier to grant the soul physical extension than to explain how something without extension could move anything. Descartes dedicated his *Principles of Philosophy* to her. He never answered her question.

Machiavelli had separated politics from inherited moral authority. Descartes performed the same operation on knowledge itself: he removed tradition, scripture, and the testimony of the senses and asked what a single mind could establish from nothing. The promise is exhilarating. You need no priest, no professor, no institution to tell you what is true. Descartes did his best work in self-imposed exile, insulated from the authorities who had silenced Galileo. When he finally accepted an invitation from Queen Christina of Sweden, who wanted philosophy lessons at five in the morning in a freezing castle library, he lasted four months before dying of pneumonia at fifty-three.

You have probably had the experience of sitting alone, stripping away the opinions you inherited and the assumptions you absorbed, and trying to figure out what you actually believe. That exercise owes something to this man in a heated room. But when you reached your conclusion, was it really your own? Or did the quiet room, the freedom from interruption, the luxury of time to think clearly, do the work for you? And if someone without that room, without that freedom, reached a different conclusion, would yours still hold?
