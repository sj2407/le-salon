# Nietzsche — The Death of God

In 1879, Friedrich Nietzsche resigned his professorship at the University of Basel. He was thirty-four, nearly blind, and suffering migraines that confined him to bed for days at a time. He had held the chair in classical philology since he was twenty-four, the youngest person ever appointed to it. Now he had no job, no citizenship, and almost no readers. For the next decade he moved between boarding houses in Nice, Sils-Maria, Turin, and Genoa, writing a book nearly every year, each one circling the same question: European morality was built on Christian foundations, those foundations were collapsing, and no one had faced what comes after.

He was not the first to see the collapse. Nietzsche wrote during the consolidation of Bismarck's German Empire, unified by war in 1871 and held together through Kulturkampf between Protestants and Catholics, suppression of Social Democrats, and rapid industrialization. Across Europe, the intellectual ground beneath Christian belief had been giving way for decades. Darwin had replaced divine creation with natural selection. David Strauss had treated the Gospels as historical documents rather than revealed truth. Ludwig Feuerbach had argued that God was not humanity's creator but humanity's creation, a projection of human needs onto the sky. The philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer, whom Nietzsche discovered as a student in Leipzig in the 1860s, drew what he considered the honest conclusion from all of this: existence is driven by a blind, purposeless will, and the wisest response is to withdraw from wanting anything at all. Nietzsche thought Schopenhauer had identified the problem correctly but surrendered too soon. The composer Richard Wagner, Nietzsche's closest intellectual companion through his twenties, attempted a different response: rebuilding cultural meaning through art. Nietzsche's first book, *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872), championed Wagner's project. But by 1878 he had broken with Wagner, convinced that Wagner's late operas, above all *Parsifal*, were not moving beyond Christianity but retreating into it, wrapping a story of Christian redemption in Germanic mythology. Marx, who died in 1883 while Nietzsche was still writing, had argued that the dominant moral and political ideas of any society are tools of whichever class controls production. Nietzsche extended that suspicion beyond economics: morality itself, including the morality of Marx's revolutionaries, serves the interests of a particular human type.

In *The Gay Science* (1882), he staged the confrontation. A madman runs through a marketplace carrying a lantern in broad daylight, crying "I seek God!" The atheists in the crowd laugh at him. Then he turns: "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him." Nietzsche's point is not that atheism is correct. The atheists already know that. His point is that they have not understood what follows. For two millennia, Christian theology gave European morality its structure: compassion was a duty because God commanded it, guilt was a discipline because God judged it, human equality made sense because every soul stood equal before God. Remove the theology and these values do not simply persist on their own. They lose their foundation. The madman has come too soon. His audience cannot yet feel the void beneath their feet.

If Christian values are not eternal truths, where did they come from? Nietzsche's answer, in *On the Genealogy of Morality* (1887), is a history of moral feeling. He identified two patterns. In what he called "master morality," the powerful and confident define themselves as good and regard the weak with indifference, not hatred. In "slave morality," the powerless cannot defeat their masters by force, so they perform a creative act of redefinition: strength becomes evil, weakness becomes virtue, and suffering becomes proof of spiritual worth. Nietzsche named this mechanism *ressentiment*, a creative resentment through which the powerless do not aspire to surpass the strong but to make strength itself sinful. Christianity, he argued, universalized this redefinition. Humility, pity, self-denial, obedience: these were the values of people who lacked the power to live otherwise, elevated into sacred law.

What replaces the sacred law once it is exposed? Nietzsche saw two possibilities. The first is nihilism: if no values are given, then nothing matters, and paralysis or destruction follow. The second is creation: a human being who, instead of inheriting meaning from God or tradition, generates it from within. Nietzsche called this figure the Übermensch. The drive that powers such creation he called the will to power, which is not a desire to dominate other people but a drive to master and shape yourself, to impose a form on your own existence that you chose rather than received. And the test of whether you have succeeded is what Nietzsche called the eternal recurrence: imagine you were told your life, every detail of it, would repeat identically forever. Could you want that? If you would recoil, you are still living according to values you borrowed. If you could affirm it, you have made something that stands on its own.

Nietzsche was almost unread during his sane lifetime. *Beyond Good and Evil* sold a few hundred copies when he published it himself in 1886. The first public recognition came from the Danish critic Georg Brandes, who lectured on Nietzsche's philosophy at the University of Copenhagen in 1888, describing it as "aristocratic radicalism." Nietzsche, in one of his last lucid letters, called this the cleverest thing anyone had written about him. Months later, in January 1889, he collapsed in Turin and never recovered his mind. He lived another eleven years, silent and cared for, first by his mother, then by his sister Elisabeth. Elisabeth had married a prominent antisemite named Bernhard Förster, had helped found an Aryan colony in Paraguay, and after Nietzsche's collapse took control of his literary archive. She edited his notebooks selectively, publishing fragments under the title *The Will to Power* as though they were the masterwork he had planned. She hosted Hitler at the archive; when she died in 1935, stormtroopers lined the road to the cemetery. Nietzsche had written openly against antisemitism and German nationalism in his published works and had refused to attend his sister's wedding over her husband's politics. But his language of strength, overcoming, and the Übermensch, stripped from its philosophical context, proved easy to conscript for purposes he would have despised. The scholarly recovery began in the 1950s with Walter Kaufmann's translations, which reframed Nietzsche as a psychologist of morality rather than a prophet of domination, and was completed by the critical edition of Colli and Montinari in the 1960s, which restored the original texts and exposed Elisabeth's distortions.

The misappropriation, though, exposes a problem that the scholarly correction does not resolve. Bertrand Russell argued in *A History of Western Philosophy* that Nietzsche's rejection of universal moral principles left his philosophy with no defense against its own misuse: if there is no standard that applies to everyone, there is no ground on which to say the Nazis got Nietzsche wrong, only that they got him differently. Nietzsche would respond that the distinction between the Übermensch and the tyrant is the distinction between creating from strength and seizing from resentment, between someone who has mastered themselves and someone who merely dominates others. Russell's objection is that this distinction has no referee. The tyrant never recognizes himself as acting from resentment. Every strongman believes his will is creative, not compensatory. Without a principle external to the individual, you cannot tell the difference from the inside. You have dropped a commitment you once honored, a loyalty, a rule you lived by, and called it self-overcoming. You told yourself the old obligation was inherited, not chosen, and that shedding it made you more honest. Were you overcoming yourself, or were you simply doing what was easier and finding a philosophy that made it sound like growth?
