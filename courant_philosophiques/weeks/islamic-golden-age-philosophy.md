# Islamic Golden Age Philosophy

In the year 980, a boy named Ibn Sina was born near Bukhara, in what is now Uzbekistan. By his own account he had memorized the Quran by age ten and exhausted his teachers in medicine by sixteen. At eighteen, a local ruler fell gravely ill, and his court physicians, unable to help, sent for the young man whose reputation had already spread across the region. Ibn Sina cured the ruler. His reward was access to the royal library of the Samanid dynasty, a collection so vast he later wrote that he had never seen its equal. He read everything in it.

The library existed because of a decision made two centuries earlier. When the Abbasid caliphate consolidated power in Baghdad in the mid-eighth century, it set about translating the intellectual inheritance of the ancient world into Arabic on an imperial scale. Within a century, nearly the entire corpus of Aristotle, substantial works of Plato, Galen, and Ptolemy, and treatises from Indian and Persian traditions had been rendered into Arabic, many by Syriac-speaking Christian translators. The Latin West, which had lost access to most of Aristotle after the fall of Rome, would not recover these texts for another three hundred years, and only then through Arabic intermediaries.

What the Islamic philosophers did with this inheritance was not preservation. It was transformation. Al-Kindi, the first major Arabic philosopher, had argued in the ninth century that truth is universal and that Greek philosophy should be welcomed wherever it leads. Avicenna, as Ibn Sina became known in the Latin West, took that principle and built from it a complete philosophical system. His driving question was the relationship between what a thing is and that it exists. For any created thing, these are separate: a horse's nature does not require that any horse actually exist. Only one being's essence is existence itself, a being that cannot not exist. Avicenna called this the Necessary Existent, and from it everything else in the universe flows by rational necessity.

He made the boldest consequences vivid with a thought experiment he called the Flying Man. Imagine a person created fully formed in midair, blindfolded, limbs separated so they touch nothing, with no sensory input of any kind. Would this person be aware of his own existence? Avicenna insisted the answer is yes. The soul knows itself before it knows the world. Strip away every sensation, every memory, every bodily feeling, and something still remains: the awareness of being. Six centuries before Descartes wrote "I think, therefore I am," Avicenna had reached the same shore and drawn a more radical consequence. If the soul is aware of itself without any bodily input, then the body is not part of what the soul is. The mind is not the brain. Consciousness is not a byproduct of matter.

This was philosophy conducted with the confidence that reason and revelation describe the same reality. Averroes, writing in twelfth-century Cordoba, made the case explicitly. In his Decisive Treatise he argued, as a judge issuing a legal ruling, that the study of philosophy is obligatory for those with the capacity for it. His principle was blunt: truth does not contradict truth. If a philosophical demonstration conflicts with the apparent meaning of scripture, scripture must be interpreted allegorically. Philosophy and religion are two languages for the same truth, calibrated for audiences of different intellectual capacity.

Al-Ghazali, the most formidable critic of the philosophers, refused this settlement. In his Incoherence of the Philosophers, written in 1095, he attacked Avicenna on twenty points, three of which he declared outright apostasy: that the world is eternal rather than created in time, that God knows only universals and not individual particulars, and that bodily resurrection is impossible. His deepest argument struck at causation itself. When fire touches cotton, al-Ghazali wrote, God burns the cotton. The fire is not the cause. We observe regularity, not necessity, and God is free to break any pattern at any moment. Reason, pushed to its limit, arrives not at certainty but at the recognition of its own dependence on a power that exceeds it.

Averroes answered him point by point in The Incoherence of the Incoherence, but the larger argument was already shifting. In the Sunni Muslim world, the tradition of falsafa receded, absorbed into theology and mysticism. Averroes's commentaries on Aristotle, meanwhile, traveled to Paris and Oxford in Latin translation, where they shaped Thomas Aquinas, who referred to Averroes simply as "The Commentator." The fact that Aquinas could write the Summa at all depended on Arabic translations of texts the Christian West had lost. The transmission line runs from Athens to Baghdad to Cordoba to Paris, and it was Arabic-speaking thinkers who kept the signal alive.

If you believe your deepest convictions can withstand rational scrutiny, would you welcome the test, as Averroes did, and allow that any apparent conflict between your faith and your reasoning demands reinterpretation of the faith? Or would you side with al-Ghazali and insist that reason, however brilliant, eventually runs out, and that the most honest philosophy is the one that knows where to stop?

— Averroes (Ibn Rushd), *Decisive Treatise*

**Further reading:**
- Averroes, *Decisive Treatise* (philosophy as legal argument for the compatibility of reason and revelation)
- Al-Ghazali, *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* (the most influential critique of Aristotelian philosophy in the Islamic world)
- Avicenna, *The Book of Healing* (a complete philosophical system spanning logic, natural science, mathematics, and metaphysics)
- Peter Adamson, *Philosophy in the Islamic World* (a lucid modern introduction)
