# Kierkegaard: The Leap of Faith

In September 1840, Søren Kierkegaard proposed to Regine Olsen. She accepted. He later wrote in his journal that the next day he saw he had made a blunder. For the next year he agonized, then sent her back the ring with a note: "Forgive a man who, though he may be capable of something, is not capable of making a girl happy." She begged him to stay. He refused. In his journal he wrote that when the bond broke, he saw two paths: wild dissipation or absolute religiousness. He chose the second, left for Berlin, and began writing the books that would make him one of the most consequential thinkers of the nineteenth century. He never married. He never fully explained why.

Copenhagen in the 1840s was saturated with Hegel. His system held the Danish intellectual class in a tight grip, reinforced by H.L. Martensen, who would become Bishop Primate of the Danish People's Church. Hegel had promised that reason and spirit were moving toward a synthesis in which every contradiction could be resolved. The individual's task was to recognize his place within the rational whole. The Danish state church had made the faith comfortable and automatic: citizens were Christians by birth through compulsory baptism and church taxes, without being asked what Christianity required of them. Kierkegaard believed the church had turned a life to be lived into a doctrine to be taught.

Like Nietzsche after him, Kierkegaard saw the comfortable systems of his day as enemies of authentic life. Both insisted on the individual against the crowd. But where Nietzsche responded to the collapse of religious authority by demanding the creation of new values, Kierkegaard responded by insisting that faith, real faith, had never been tried. His starting point was not strength but anxiety. Before you can create anything, he argued, you have to reckon with a fact most people spend their lives avoiding: you have not yet become a self.

Kierkegaard described three spheres of existence. In the aesthetic sphere, you live for pleasure and novelty, collecting experiences without commitment. The aesthete drifts from sensation to sensation and calls it freedom, but Kierkegaard saw this life as boredom concealing despair. In the ethical sphere, you live by duty: you marry, you work, you fulfill your social roles. Judge William, the fictional spokesman for this stage in *Either/Or*, argues that commitment is what gives a life shape. But the ethical sphere has its own limit. You can do everything your society expects of you and still feel that something essential is missing, that the roles you inhabit are not the same thing as being a self. The religious sphere is what follows when duty runs out of answers. Here, the individual stands alone, stripped of social identity and rational justification, before a question that no system can resolve for him.

No argument carries you from one sphere to the next. The transition requires what Kierkegaard called a leap. Kierkegaard described the dread that accompanies this moment as the dizziness of freedom: the vertigo you feel when you look down into the open space of your own possibilities and realize that nothing forces your hand. Most people, he argued, never make the leap. They run from the dizziness into what he called despair, a condition he compared to a disease that produces no symptoms. You can be in despair and appear perfectly content. You can build a career, maintain a family, earn the respect of your neighbors, and still be, in Kierkegaard's terms, sick. "The greatest hazard of all, losing one's self, can occur very quietly in the world, as if it were nothing at all," he wrote in *The Sickness Unto Death*. You lose your phone and notice immediately. You lose yourself and may not notice for years.

In *Fear and Trembling*, published in 1843 under the pseudonym Johannes de Silentio, Kierkegaard pushed the leap to its most extreme form through a retelling of Abraham and Isaac. God commands Abraham to sacrifice his son. By every ethical standard, this is murder. A tragic hero like Agamemnon can sacrifice his daughter for the welfare of a nation and remain within the ethical: his act serves a communal purpose that others can recognize. Abraham has no such recourse. His act is purely private, a relation between himself and God that no one else can understand or justify. Kierkegaard called this the teleological suspension of the ethical: the claim that something exists above the universal moral law. Abraham is the limit case, the point where Kierkegaard's philosophy reveals what it demands at its most radical: a willingness to act on something you cannot prove, cannot explain, and cannot defend to anyone else.

If the single individual can override the moral law by claiming a private relation to the absolute, what prevents anyone from doing the same? Adorno, writing his study of Kierkegaard in the early 1930s as fascism consolidated in Germany, argued that Kierkegaard's inwardness was a hollow retreat. The isolated subject, enclosed in what Adorno called the "bourgeois interior," mistakes his private spiritual drama for the highest form of existence while the social world that produced him reshapes itself without his participation. Inwardness claims to transcend society, but it depends on the very conditions it refuses to examine. Kierkegaard insists that you must look inward to find yourself. Adorno replies that the self you find there was assembled by forces you never chose and cannot see from inside.

You have, at some point, described yourself using the things you do. Your job, your relationships, the city you live in, the tastes you have cultivated. Kierkegaard would ask what remains if you strip all of that away. Not as a thought experiment but as a real question with weight behind it: if the career ended, the relationship dissolved, the routines broke, would you find a self underneath, or would you find the void those things were covering? Most of us suspect we know the answer, which is why we avoid the question. Kierkegaard would say the avoidance is the disease. But Adorno would ask something harder: when you do look inward and believe you have found your true self beneath the roles, how do you know that self is not just another product of the same world you think you are escaping?
