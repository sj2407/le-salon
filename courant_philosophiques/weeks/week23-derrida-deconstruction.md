Derrida and Deconstruction

On 21 October 1966, at Johns Hopkins University, a thirty-six-year-old Algerian-born philosopher named Jacques Derrida read a paper called "La structure, le signe et le jeu dans le discours des sciences humaines" ("Structure, Sign, and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences"). He was a last-minute substitute for an absent Belgian anthropologist and had written the lecture in less than two weeks. The conference, "The Languages of Criticism and the Sciences of Man," was organized by the new Humanities Center to introduce American academics to structuralism, the French intellectual movement of the previous decade. Roland Barthes, Jacques Lacan, and Jean Hyppolite, Derrida's old teacher, were there. By the end of the conference, structuralism had been overtaken by what would soon be called post-structuralism, with Derrida's lecture cited as its founding document.

Structuralism, founded by Ferdinand de Saussure's early-twentieth-century lectures on linguistics and extended into anthropology, literature, and psychoanalysis by Claude Lévi-Strauss, Barthes, and Lacan, held that meaning is produced by relations between elements of a system rather than by any individual mind. A word means what it means because of how it differs from other words. The individual subject is a product of the system, not its source. The pitch of structuralism was that meaning required no foundation outside the system: no God, no consciousness behind the signs.

Derrida agreed that the individual subject is not the source of meaning. But every actual structuralist analysis, he argued, quietly relied on a foundation it officially denied. He called this hidden foundation a "center": a privileged term such as God, presence, man, or structure itself, which anchors the system without being defined by the system's rules. The center has to sit outside the play of differences to ground them. Once you notice this, structuralism's claim to have dissolved metaphysics collapses. It had inherited the same demand for a fixed point that the philosophical tradition had always relied on.

Born in 1930 in El-Biar near Algiers into a Sephardic Jewish family, Derrida was expelled from school under Vichy. He moved to Paris after the war and studied phenomenology under Hyppolite. In 1967 he published three books in a single year: *Of Grammatology*, *Writing and Difference*, and *Voice and Phenomenon*. He had borrowed the word *deconstruction* from Heidegger's *Destruktion*, the dismantling of inherited philosophical concepts. He used it almost in passing, as a working description of his method. Within a decade his readers had made it the name for an entire approach.

The target of those books was what Derrida called the metaphysics of presence. Western philosophy, on his reading, has consistently treated meaning as something that should ideally be fully present to a self-conscious subject: God's word delivered directly to the prophet, the soul's inner monologue with itself, the speaker's intention behind the spoken word. Within this scheme, writing has always been the second-class case. Speech, the tradition argued, is closer to thought. Writing detaches from the writer. Once written, a text can be read after the author has died, and misread with no one to correct it.

In Plato's *Phaedrus*, Socrates tells a myth in which the Egyptian god Theuth offers writing to King Thamus, who refuses it: writing will make people forgetful, will replace living teachers with dead letters, is a *pharmakon*, both remedy and poison. Saussure declared writing an "external system" with no business inside linguistics; the real object of study was the spoken word. Rousseau, for whom speech was the voice of natural feeling, treated writing as a dangerous *supplement*, an addition that ended up replacing what it was supposed to enhance, turning living expression into dead inscription.

Derrida's response was that this hierarchy gets the structure backwards. The features the tradition attributes to writing (absence of the author, dependence on context, inability to control reception) are features of language as such. Saussure had already shown that signs signify by their differences from other signs: "cat" means cat partly because it isn't "bat" or "rat." Derrida pushed the consequence further. Meaning is also deferred across time, suspended by what comes next in the sentence, in future contexts. He coined a term for this double movement: *différance*, spelled with an *a* where the standard French *différence* has an *e*. Even in internal monologue, where I seem to hear myself think, there is a minimal split between the part of me that speaks and the part of me that hears. The full self-presence the tradition treats as the original case of meaning has never existed.

The line most associated with Derrida is also the most misread: *il n'y a pas de hors-texte*, usually translated as "there is nothing outside the text." Critics have read him as denying the world. He meant nothing of the kind. The world exists. There are wars, prisons, bodies. Nothing reaches us unmediated. Every fact, every object, every experience comes to us through a system of signs that shapes what we can register about it.

In "Signature Event Context" (1971), Derrida named this feature *iterability*. A written mark works as a sign only because it can be repeated. The word "cat" written on this page must be the same word when written elsewhere, by someone the original writer never met. If a sign could only function in its original context, for its original reader, with its original intention attached, it would not be a sign at all. The possibility of being lifted from its moment, cited, parodied, misread, used by a stranger, is not a defect of writing. It is the condition that makes any sign function.

The same condition holds for speech. When you say "I'll be there," the words mean what they mean because they can be repeated. Your intention does not travel with the sentence. The listener has access only to the words, which are doing what signs always do: differing from other words, deferring their meaning to context. The speaker is not the master of what their words mean once spoken. The gap between what is said and what is heard is not a failure of communication. For Derrida, it is what communication is.

The most substantial objection to this comes from a philosopher who never wrote about Derrida, whose framework cuts directly against him. In *Philosophical Investigations* (1953), the Austrian-born Cambridge philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein argued that meaning is not a hidden thing behind words. It is what people do with them. Words are tools. Their meaning is fixed by use within a *form of life*, a shared practice in which speakers and listeners have public criteria for whether something has been said well, badly, ironically, sincerely. The British philosopher Stephen Mulhall, in "Wittgenstein and Deconstruction," has brought this position directly against Derrida.

The Wittgensteinian objection runs like this. Yes, words can be cited, parodied, misread, lifted from their original context. Yes, the speaker has no private guarantee that the listener received what they meant. But these are observations about the limits of philosophical certainty, not the structure of communication. In ordinary practice, meaning gets fixed. You ask what someone meant; they tell you. You misread a message; the other person clarifies. When you and someone close to you exchange a difficult message, you are not two strangers trading context-free signs. You share years of conversation, mutual knowledge of each other's patterns, ways of checking and repairing. The structural possibility of misreading that Derrida treats as the condition of language is closed, in practice, by the work of being in a relationship. Derrida has mistaken the philosopher's anxiety about meaning for the truth about meaning, and a real but marginal feature of signs for the structure of communication itself.

You read a difficult message from someone close to you. You re-read it and the meaning shifts. You compose a reply, delete it, compose another, send it. The moment you send it, you read it back and it is not quite what you meant. The other person will read it differently. They will read it next week and read it differently. Somewhere in this you decided, but you cannot say what you were doing when you made it. No rule of communication closes the gap between what was said and what was heard.

Derrida wants you to recognize this gap as the condition of language, not a failure of any particular speaker. Wittgenstein, and Mulhall after him, want you to recognize the gap as a problem you and the other person work to close, every day, in the practice of staying in a relationship. Is the gap the structure of meaning, or is it what care and conversation are for?
