# Structuralism

In 1936, a young French professor named Claude Lévi-Strauss stood at the edge of a Bororo village in the Brazilian interior and drew a diagram. The huts were arranged in a circle around a central men's house, and the pattern looked random until he began asking who lived where. An invisible axis divided the village into two halves. Within each half, clans ranked themselves in three tiers. Marriage was permitted only between halves and only within the same tier. When a man married, he crossed the clearing to live among his wife's kin. The funeral rites of one half were organized by the other. Every obligation and prohibition was encoded in the physical layout of the village, and none of the Bororo needed to articulate these rules to follow them. They inhabited a structure the way a speaker inhabits grammar: fluently and unconsciously. From that diagram, Lévi-Strauss built the theory that would displace existentialism, because the Bororo had shown him something most Westerners refused to believe about themselves: that social life runs on inherited rules that feel like common sense to the people inside them and look like arbitrary choices to anyone outside.

To understand what "structure" means here, think of a chessboard. A pawn is a piece of wood with no meaning in isolation. It becomes a pawn through its position relative to other pieces and the rules governing what it can do. Replace it with a bottle cap and nothing about the game changes, because the pawn's identity is entirely relational. This is what the structuralists meant by a "system of differences": the elements of a system do not carry meaning in themselves, only through their relationships to every other element.

The idea that the mind filters and interprets experience rather than passively receiving it was not new. Kant had argued in the eighteenth century that the mind does not receive the world as it is but shapes it through built-in filters like space, time, and causality. Marx had claimed that social being determines consciousness: your position in the economic structure shapes what you can think, want, and imagine. The Frankfurt School took Marx further and argued that the culture industry manufactures the desires people mistake for their own. Structuralism radicalized the claim: it is not just economics or ideology but the entire system of signs and categories that produces the thinking subject. You do not exist underneath the codes and then assemble them. The codes assembled you.

The technical foundation came from Ferdinand de Saussure, a Swiss linguist who never published his most consequential ideas himself. His students compiled their lecture notes after his death in 1913 and published them in 1916 as the Course in General Linguistics. Saussure made two claims. First, a word does not get its meaning from the thing it refers to. The word "tree" means what it means because it is not "free," not "three," not "trap." Meaning arises from difference within a system. Second, this system, which Saussure called "langue," operates independently of any individual speaker.

Structuralism arrived in a France undergoing its own transformation. The Fourth Republic collapsed in 1958 under the weight of the Algerian War. De Gaulle's Fifth Republic was rebuilding through centralized planning and rapid industrialization while American consumer culture flooded in. The Catholic Church was losing its grip on the educated classes. Universities were expanding, and a new generation of students found Sartre's wartime existentialism, with its emphasis on heroic individual choice, increasingly distant from the administered society they inhabited. Structuralism offered a way to study the codes that organized modern life without relying on Sartre's faith in individual freedom or the Church's faith in divine order.

Saussure's ideas had reshaped linguistics, but it took a chance encounter during the war to carry them into the study of culture. Lévi-Strauss met the linguist Roman Jakobson while both were wartime refugees at the New School for Social Research in New York. Jakobson had shown that the sounds of a language can be analyzed as a system of binary contrasts: voiced and voiceless, nasal and oral. Lévi-Strauss recognized that if this worked for language, it could work for culture. He tried it on kinship systems first, demonstrating in The Elementary Structures of Kinship (1949) that marriage rules that look different across societies are rearrangements of a few underlying patterns. At the center of every kinship system he studied, he found the same mechanism: a group drawing a line between those you may marry and those you may not. The incest taboo was his test case. Every known human society prohibits sexual relations between certain kin, which makes the prohibition look biological. But which relatives count as "too close" varies enormously, which makes it look cultural. Lévi-Strauss argued this is the founding act of culture itself: the moment a group says "we will exchange partners with outsiders rather than reproduce among ourselves" is the moment nature becomes society.

He then turned to mythology. In The Raw and the Cooked (1964), he argued that myths from unrelated cultures transform the same set of binary oppositions: nature and culture, raw and cooked, life and death. "Raw" does not describe a property of food. It exists as a concept only because "cooked" exists, and together they form one of the codes through which a culture draws the line between the animal and the human. Different cultures fill the opposition with different content, but the opposition itself persists.

Roland Barthes showed that the same structural logic operates in the most ordinary objects of consumer society. In Mythologies (1957), he treated professional wrestling, magazine covers, and consumer goods as symbols that communicate messages about the social order. His most pointed example was a cover of Paris Match showing a young Black soldier in French uniform saluting the tricolor. The image carries a second-order message beneath the literal one: that France is a great empire, faithfully served by all its subjects regardless of race, and that this arrangement is natural. Barthes called this second-order operation "myth." Myth works by transforming history into nature. When a politician is photographed eating steak-frites, the image signals "I am of the people, authentically French" in contrast to foods that would mark foreignness or pretension. You intuitively understand both the symbol and the message behind it.

By the early 1960s, structuralism had displaced existentialism as the dominant intellectual current in Paris. Its adherents were concentrated in the Parisian academic elite. Foucault, Lacan, Althusser, and Barthes were all working out the consequences of a single proposition: the individual subject does not produce meaning but is produced by it. This is not cultural relativism, the banal observation that different societies have different customs. It is the claim that there is no self underneath the customs. Your sense of what is logical, what is funny, what counts as a reasonable thing to want from life: all were installed by the system of signs you grew up inside.

Sartre saw the moral stakes and refused the position. In the Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960), he insisted that structures are real but that they are made and remade by the activity of human beings. The structuralists, he charged, had produced a philosophy that could describe a society but could never judge one. If all moral convictions are products of a particular structure, then you can say "my structure prohibits cannibalism" but you cannot say "cannibalism is wrong," because "wrong" is itself a category the structure produced.

There was a second weakness, equally serious. Structuralism could not explain historical change. Saussure had prioritized synchronic analysis (the system at a given moment) over diachronic analysis (how systems change over time). Lévi-Strauss mostly avoided history. But France itself had gone from Catholic monarchy to secular republic. If the structure determines what you can think, and you cannot think beyond the structure, then who noticed the contradictions? Who acted on them? In October 1966, Jacques Derrida delivered a lecture at Johns Hopkins University called "Structure, Sign and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences" that formalized this problem. Every structure, Derrida argued, presupposes a center that organizes it and limits the play of its elements. But that center cannot be explained by the structure it governs. The concept of "structure" is unstable by the structuralists' own logic. The conference had been organized to consolidate structuralism as a paradigm. Derrida's lecture marked the beginning of what would be called post-structuralism.

You hold preferences that feel entirely your own: what you find attractive, what you consider a successful life, what strikes you as obviously true. Structuralism asks you to consider that every one of those instincts is a product of the particular system of differences you were born into, and that in a different structure the things you find obvious would be unintelligible and the things you desire would not even occur to you. The question is not whether you can accept this as an abstract proposition. Most people can. The question is whether anything changes when you do.
