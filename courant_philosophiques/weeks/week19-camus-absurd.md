# Camus and the Absurd

In September 1941, Albert Camus packed a novel and a philosophical essay into a parcel and mailed them to a publisher in Paris from the city of Oran, Algeria. He was twenty-seven. He had returned to Algeria after fleeing the German invasion of France the previous year, and was teaching at a private school to make ends meet. He had been fighting tuberculosis since he was seventeen. His father had died of shrapnel at the Battle of the Marne before Camus turned one. His mother, partially deaf and nearly illiterate, had raised him in a three-room apartment in the Belcourt district of Algiers, with no electricity and no running water. The novel was *The Stranger*. The essay was *The Myth of Sisyphus*. Together they announced a single, unanswerable question: if the universe says nothing back to us, what reason is there to keep living?

That question was not rhetorical. Camus placed it at the start of *The Myth of Sisyphus* with a bluntness that still startles: the only truly serious philosophical problem is suicide. He did not mean this as provocation. He meant it as a logical starting point. If life has no inherent meaning, if the universe meets our deepest questions with silence, then the decision to continue living requires justification. Everything else in philosophy comes after that.

The week before, Sartre had offered one answer: existence precedes essence, meaning is something you create through committed action. Camus rejected this. He agreed with Sartre that there is no predetermined purpose, no God-given script. But he refused the next step. You cannot manufacture meaning in an indifferent universe. You can act, you can commit, you can build, but the silence remains. The world does not become meaningful because you have decided it should be. The gap between what you need from the universe and what the universe provides is permanent. Camus gave this gap a name: the absurd.

The absurd, as Camus defined it, is not a property of the world alone or of the human mind alone. It arises in the collision between the two. Human beings are built to seek purpose, order, coherence. The universe offers none. Neither side can eliminate the other. You cannot stop wanting meaning any more than the universe can start providing it. The absurd is this confrontation, held open, never resolved.

Camus then surveyed the available escapes and rejected each one. Religion is the most ancient: it resolves the absurd by positing a meaning beyond this life, a God who answers the silence. Camus called this "philosophical suicide," a leap that kills the question instead of facing it. He extended the same judgment to the thinkers who had come closest to facing the absurd: Kierkegaard, Jaspers, Heidegger. Each, at the decisive moment, leaps into transcendence or faith. They feel the absurd and then smuggle hope back in through the side door. Camus wanted to stay in the room.

What remains when you refuse both suicide and hope? Camus answered with an image. Sisyphus, condemned by the gods to push a boulder up a mountain only to watch it roll back down, then to descend and begin again, forever. The labor is pointless. There will be no final summit. Sisyphus knows this. And Camus insists that we must imagine Sisyphus happy. Not because the task secretly has meaning, not because endurance itself becomes a purpose, but because Sisyphus has stopped waiting for the rock to stay at the top. He lives inside the absurd without trying to resolve it. His happiness comes from the clarity of a man who has given up on appeals to a higher court and who fills his life with the weight and texture of the present moment: the rough surface of the stone, the strain of muscle, the descent in open air.

This was not where Camus stopped. In 1951, he published *The Rebel*, and the question shifted from "should I kill myself?" to "should I kill others?" If life is absurd and all values are human inventions, does anything prevent the strong from crushing the weak? Camus answered with a distinction that cost him his closest friendship. A rebel says no to oppression while affirming a limit: there are things one will not do even in the service of justice. A revolutionary, by contrast, sacrifices the present to a vision of the future and accepts any violence that serves that vision.

Sartre read *The Rebel* as a betrayal. In his journal *Les Temps Modernes*, he arranged a scathing review by Francis Jeanson, then published his own reply when Camus responded. The issue sold out three times. Sartre accused Camus of retreating into moral purity while real people suffered under colonial and capitalist oppression. If workers are trapped, if colonized peoples are brutalized, then revolutionary violence is the price of their liberation, and to refuse that price is to side with the oppressors by default. Camus was a "beautiful soul" who preferred clean hands to dirty history.

Camus saw a different danger. He had traced a specific lineage in *The Rebel*: the French Revolution began with the rights of man and ended with the Terror; the Russian Revolution began with the liberation of the working class and ended with the Soviet labor camps. The pattern was not accidental. Revolutionary violence, undertaken in the name of a future paradise, reproduced tyranny because it accepted no limit on what could be sacrificed to reach that paradise. Camus had seen a smaller version of this himself. As editor of *Combat* during the Liberation, he had initially supported the purges of Nazi collaborators, then watched them become indiscriminate, mob-driven, impossible to contain. That experience turned him toward the question at the center of *The Rebel*: once you accept that killing can be redeemed by its political outcome, there is no floor beneath you. The rebel holds that floor in place by refusing to cross it, even when the cause is just. Sartre's accusation cut deep partly because it touched something real: Camus, born in Algeria to a *pied-noir* family, never endorsed Algerian independence outright, and his calls for reconciliation between colonizers and colonized looked increasingly detached from the violence on the ground. He died in a car accident in January 1960, at forty-six, before the war ended.

Sartre's critique exposes a tension Camus answered but never fully closed. Camus did offer a foundation for the rebel's limit: the act of rebellion itself. When a person says no to oppression, that refusal simultaneously affirms a shared human dignity that applies to everyone, not just the rebel. "I rebel, therefore we exist," he wrote, inverting Descartes. Rebellion reveals solidarity as a fact discovered in action, not a theory imposed from above. Sartre found this insufficient. Solidarity discovered through refusal does not tell you what to do when solidarity requires violence.

Camus did not leave the question abstract. He traced it to the most concrete institution he could find: the death penalty. In "Reflections on the Guillotine," he argued that capital punishment is the clearest case where a society crosses the rebel's limit while believing it is upholding it. The state kills to protect human life. It violates the very value it claims to defend. The logic mirrors what he had identified in the Terror and the camps: a premise rooted in the protection of the innocent, transformed into premeditated killing in the name of justice. The subject was not academic for him. One of the few things Camus ever learned about his father was that he had gone to watch a public execution expecting to see justice done, and had come home unable to speak, vomiting for hours. He had seen what the state's justice looked like up close, and the sight made him ill.

You have probably formed a view on capital punishment. But Camus is asking something more specific than whether you support or oppose it. He is asking whether you have ever wanted someone punished so severely that the punishment would require crossing a line you believe in. Whether you have felt that a person's act was so destructive that the only adequate response would mirror the destruction. And if you have, whether you noticed, in that moment, that your desire for justice had begun to speak in the same voice as the violence it was meant to answer.
