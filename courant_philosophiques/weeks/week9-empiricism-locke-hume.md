# Empiricism: Locke and Hume

In 1734, a twenty-three-year-old Scotsman named David Hume arrived at La Flèche, a quiet town in the Loire Valley known for one thing: its Jesuit college, where René Descartes had studied over a century before. Hume had dropped out of Edinburgh at fifteen, abandoned a law career, suffered a nervous breakdown from the intensity of his reading, and failed as a merchant's clerk in Bristol. He came to La Flèche with no credentials and no prospects, only the conviction that he had found something Locke and Descartes had both missed. He spent four years there writing *A Treatise of Human Nature*. It fell, as he later said, "dead-born from the press, without reaching such distinction as even to excite a murmur among the zealots."

Hume was finishing what John Locke had started half a century earlier. Locke, a physician and political exile who fled England for the Netherlands in 1683, had published his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* in 1689, the year after the Glorious Revolution brought him home. His target was Descartes. Where Descartes had claimed that certain ideas are innate, built into the mind by God before any experience occurs, Locke argued that the mind at birth is a blank slate, a *tabula rasa*. Every idea we possess comes from sensation or reflection. There are no exceptions. If you cannot trace an idea back to some experience, it has no legitimate claim on your belief.

Hume took Locke's principle and applied it with a ruthlessness Locke never attempted. If every idea must come from experience, then test the idea of causation. Watch one billiard ball strike another. What do you actually see? You see the first ball move. You see the two balls make contact. You see the second ball move. You never see the causing. There is no impression of force passing from one object to another. What you call causation is a sequence you have witnessed many times, and your mind, through habit, has learned to expect. The *necessary connection* you feel between cause and effect, the sense that one *must* produce the other, is something the mind contributes. It may track something real, but you have no way to observe the connection itself, only the regularity.

Hume was not dismissing scientifically established causes. His claim was narrower and more unsettling: the certainty we attach to causal connections outruns what observation alone can deliver. Every prediction rests on the assumption that observed patterns will continue, but that assumption cannot be justified without circularity. The only reason to trust experience as a guide to the future is that experience has guided us in the past. Custom, not logic, carries us from observation to expectation.

Descartes had tried to rebuild knowledge from reason alone. Locke replied that reason without experience produces nothing. Hume completed the sequence: even experience cannot deliver the certainty we thought it promised. Immanuel Kant wrote that reading Hume woke him from his "dogmatic slumber," but Kant did not accept the conclusion. He argued that Hume had the direction wrong. Causation is not a habit the mind picks up from watching events repeat. It is a condition the mind imposes in order to have experience at all. Without the concept of cause already structuring perception, there would be no coherent experience from which to form habits in the first place. Hume's Scottish contemporary Thomas Reid pressed a different objection. Our belief in causation, Reid argued, is a first principle of common sense, as foundational as our trust in perception or memory. These beliefs do not need the kind of justification Hume demands. They are the ground you stand on to do any reasoning at all, including Hume's own.

Hume acknowledged the gap between his arguments and his life. He left his study, played backgammon, dined with friends, and trusted the world to behave as it always had. Nassim Nicholas Taleb, a former options trader, gave this problem a face in 2007: the turkey. A turkey is fed every day by the same hand. Each feeding confirms its belief that this is the general rule of life. On the one thousand and first day, the Wednesday before Thanksgiving, the hand does something else. The turkey observed, generalized, and predicted. Its method was empirically sound. Taleb argued that financial models, political forecasts, and personal assumptions all share the turkey's blind spot: the inability to account for what has not happened yet.

You have your own version of this. Some pattern you have relied on long enough that it no longer feels like a bet. It feels like a fact. Reid would say that trust is the ground beneath your feet, and that Hume's demand to justify it only leads to a paralysis no one can sustain. Hume would say you sound exactly like the turkey. The question is which one of them you are living by right now, and whether you chose it or simply never thought to ask.

**"Custom, then, is the great guide of human life. It is that principle alone which renders our experience useful to us, and makes us expect, for the future, a similar train of events with those which have appeared in the past."**
— David Hume, *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748)
