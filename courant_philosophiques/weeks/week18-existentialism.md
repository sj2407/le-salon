# Existentialism

In the winter of 1943, Jean-Paul Sartre sat at his corner table on the first floor of the Café de Flore on the Boulevard Saint-Germain, writing. German soldiers occupied the tables around him. The stove that heated the upstairs room was one of the few in the quarter that still had fuel, which is partly why he came. He wrote for three hours each morning and three each afternoon, pipe lit, filling notebooks in a cramped hand while Paris starved around him. The book he was finishing was *Being and Nothingness*. Informers sat at the next table. Anonymous letters could destroy a life. The Republic had collapsed in six weeks, the Church was divided, the Resistance was fragmented and dangerous to join. Every person in occupied Paris faced, in daily life, the condition Sartre was theorizing: no authority was reliable, no framework could tell you what the right thing to do was, and yet you had to decide each morning whether to collaborate, resist, or look away. The occupation did not create that condition. It revealed it. The Republic, the Church, the moral order had always been human constructions. Their collapse proved what the book argued: you were always free and always responsible.

France had surrendered in 1940. Sartre had been a prisoner of war, held in a camp near Trier, before returning to a Paris hollowed out by occupation. He and Simone de Beauvoir cofounded an underground group called Socialisme et Liberté, but it dissolved within months. The organized resistance they had imagined never materialized for them. What remained was writing. Phenomenology, which Sartre had studied in Berlin before the war, offered a starting point. Husserl had argued that to understand experience you must strip away the meanings layered onto it by habit and convention and study how things appear to consciousness before you categorize them. Heidegger went further: a human being is not like a chair or a stone, whose existence is settled by its properties. A chair does not ask what it is or what it should do. A human being never stops asking. Your job, your nationality, your name do not settle the question of who you are, because you can always act otherwise. Sartre took this insight and added what Heidegger had not: a moral demand. If no set of properties defines you, then you are responsible for what you become.

Sartre's answer became the defining claim of existentialism: existence precedes essence. The Western philosophical tradition from Plato through Aquinas had assumed the opposite. A paper knife is designed before it is made; its purpose comes first, its existence second. Aquinas held the same for human beings: God conceived of human nature before creating any individual person. Sartre argued that with no God to serve as designer, no blueprint for what a person should be, the order reverses. You are thrown into the world without a purpose, and whatever you become is the result of what you do. What you intend or hope counts for nothing. Only what you choose and carry out defines you. In October 1945, Sartre delivered a public lecture to explain this claim. The venue was so overcrowded that people fainted and chairs were broken. What drew them was a proposition that was either liberating or terrifying depending on whether you were willing to accept it: you are entirely responsible for what you make of yourself, and there is no one to blame.

This freedom is not a gift. Sartre called it being "condemned to be free." You did not ask to exist, but now that you do, every moment demands a choice, and refusing to choose is itself a choice you are responsible for. The flight from this condition Sartre called *mauvaise foi*, bad faith: the act of treating yourself as a fixed object rather than an open project. His most discussed example is a waiter in a café whose gestures are a little too crisp, a little too eager, as if he is performing the role of "waiter" the way an actor plays a character. The waiter is in bad faith because he behaves as though being a waiter is what he is, as though the role defines his nature rather than being something he chose and could abandon. Bad faith is comfortable because it absolves you from the responsibility of choosing. It is also a lie, one that consciousness cannot fully sustain because you always know, at some level, that you are pretending.

Simone de Beauvoir, Sartre's intellectual partner since they met at the École Normale Supérieure in 1929, took the framework and asked a question Sartre had not pursued. If there is no fixed human nature, then what about womanhood? In *The Second Sex*, published in 1949, she wrote: "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman." Femininity, she argued, is a situation, constructed through upbringing, expectation, and centuries of being defined as the Other against a male subject that passes itself off as universal. The same bad faith Sartre found in the waiter operates on a civilizational scale: an entire sex told that its essence is fixed, passive, nurturing, and subordinate. Beauvoir insisted that freedom is not an abstract concept. A woman denied education, economic independence, and control over her own body is not free in any sense that matters, whatever ontology says about consciousness. She transformed existentialism from a philosophy of individual choice into one that confronted the material structures that make choice possible or impossible.

Albert Camus, who had arrived in Paris from Algeria and sat at the same café tables, shared Sartre's diagnosis: the world offers no inherent meaning, and you cannot wait for one to arrive. Where they split was the prescription. Sartre argued that meaning is created through political commitment, that freedom demands engagement with collective struggle. In practice, this meant siding with the Soviet Union and the French Communist Party, the only organized force Sartre saw as opposing capitalist exploitation. When reports of the Gulags and show trials reached the West, Sartre did not break with the Soviets. He argued that revolutionary violence, however terrible, could be justified if it served the transition to a just society. Camus argued that this reasoning was the error. In *The Rebel* (1951), he drew a line between the rebel, who says no to injustice while affirming human limits, and the revolutionary, who sacrifices living people to an imagined future utopia. Camus supported revolt, supported trade-union socialism, supported the French war effort. What he rejected was the claim that you can kill real people now for a world that has not arrived and may never arrive. Sartre attacked Camus in the pages of *Les Temps modernes*, accusing him of retreating into moral purity while refusing to engage with political reality. Camus responded that Sartre had confused commitment with complicity. The friendship was never repaired.

Beauvoir had opened a gap that Sartre spent the rest of his career trying to close. If freedom depends on material conditions, and those conditions are shaped by structures that no individual chose, then the radical freedom of *Being and Nothingness* is incomplete. In 1960, Sartre published his second major philosophical work, *Critique of Dialectical Reason*, an attempt to show how individual freedom could ground collective action. He introduced the concept of seriality: people isolated from one another, each pursuing private goals, unable to act together. And its opposite, the group-in-fusion: people who break out of isolation and act as one, as the crowd that stormed the Bastille. Sartre had gestured at this as early as 1946, when he argued in a public lecture that to will your own freedom requires willing the freedom of others. The *Critique* tried to give that claim a philosophical foundation. Freedom no longer meant the ability to choose your attitude regardless of circumstance. It meant acting, with others, within and against the structures that constrain you.

His critics argued he did not take his philosophy to its logical conclusion. The philosopher Leszek Kołakowski pointed to what Sartre's own framework predicts: the group-in-fusion does not stay fused. It solidifies into an organized body, which hardens into an institution, which develops its own hierarchies and demands. The collective that fought to liberate becomes a new structure that constrains. Raymond Aron called the *Critique* an impossible union of Kierkegaard and Marx. Camus had raised a version of this tension a decade earlier, in political terms: the revolution that demands individual sacrifice for collective liberation ends by crushing the individuals it claimed to free. Kołakowski found the same tension lodged in Sartre's philosophy itself: any group that acts collectively will, over time, ask its members to subordinate their own judgment, their own freedom, to the group's survival. Sartre's own theory contains the stages of this hardening, but according to Kołakowski, he treated the cycle as a feature of his framework rather than a problem his framework needed to solve.

You have felt what Beauvoir described: a restriction that targets you as a member of a group, not as an individual. You have been turned down for a job you were qualified for, turned away from an apartment you could afford, because of your gender or your skin or the accent in your voice. Existentialism says you are free, that you can refuse and resist. But if you resist alone, the structure survives. So you find others, organize, act together. And the group asks something of you: loyalty, discipline, silence on the points where you disagree. At what point does the solidarity that was supposed to set you free become the thing you need to be set free from?
